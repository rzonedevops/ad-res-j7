Dan’s Understanding of the ex parte Legal Framework:

Structure of Ex Parte Interdicts: Part A and Part B

In South African civil procedure, an ex parte interdict often includes:

Part A: Urgent interim relief granted without notice to the respondent.

Part B: A return date where the respondent is given an opportunity to oppose and the matter is heard inter partes (with both parties present).

This structure is common in rule nisi applications, where:

The court grants temporary relief (Part A) based on the applicant’s affidavit.

The respondent is invited to show cause on the return date why the interim order should not be made final (Part B).

Why Part B is embedded in Part A:

The notice of motion often includes both parts to streamline the process.

Part A is granted urgently, and Part B sets the stage for a full hearing.

However, this does not mean Part B is valid if Part A was obtained fraudulently.

Engaging with Part B can:

Undermine your strongest defence (fraud vitiates everything).

Create procedural confusion by treating a void order as if it were valid.

Delay justice and allow the fraudulent party to benefit from their misconduct.

Instead, the focus must remain on proving the fraudulent nature of Part A and seeking its rescission before any further steps are taken.

Rescission of Part A as Void:

When fraud or perjury is involved in obtaining Part A, the correct legal approach is:

Rescission under Rule 42(1)(a):

This rule allows a court to rescind or vary an order:

Erroneously sought or granted in the absence of a party.

Obtained through fraud, which includes perjured affidavits or material concealment.

Void ab initio principle:

If Part A was obtained through fraud, it is void from the beginning. This means:

Part B has no legal foundation, because it arises from a void order.

Engaging with Part B (e.g., filing opposing papers) may inadvertently validate the fraudulent process.

Correct strategy:

Do not respond to Part B as if the order is valid.

File a rescission application targeting Part A, based on proven fraud.

Refer the matter for criminal investigation, especially if perjury or financial fraud is involved.

Legal Strategy in Cases of Fraud:

Why Rescission Targets Part A

When Part A of an ex parte interdict is obtained through fraud, such as perjured affidavits or material concealment, the correct legal approach is:

✅ Rescission of Part A under Rule 42(1)(a):

Purpose: To invalidate the order granted in your absence due to fraud.

Effect: The order is treated as void ab initio (from the beginning).

Focus: On the fraudulent nature of the founding papers, not the merits of the dispute.

🔍 Key Points:

No engagement with Part B: Responding to Part B implies the order is valid.

Avoid legitimizing fraud: Any procedural step that treats the order as valid undermines your strongest defence.

Criminal referral: The matter may be referred to the commercial crimes court for investigation.

📌 Strategic Steps:

File rescission application of Part A under Rule 42(1)(a).

Submit evidence of fraud/perjury.

Avoid responding to Part B.

Request criminal investigation.

Legal Strategy in Cases of Dispute:

Why Response Targets Part B

When Part A was obtained lawfully, and the issue is a civil dispute where Part B is an opportunity for parties to show cause, the correct legal approach is:

✅ Opposition to Part B under Rule 6(12)(a):

Purpose: To challenge the continuation or finalization of the interim relief.

Effect: You engage with the merits of the case and present your defence.

Focus: On disputing the claims made by the applicant.

🔍 Key Points:

Part A is presumed valid: Unless set aside, it stands as a lawful interim order.

Part B is your opportunity: To present counter-evidence and legal arguments.

Civil litigation tools apply: Discovery, affidavits, and hearings.

📌 Strategic Steps:

File opposing affidavit to Part B under Rule 6(12)(a).

Challenge the applicant’s claims on factual and legal grounds.

Request dismissal or variation of the interim order.

Prepare for inter partes hearing.
