## GROUNDS FOR APPLICATION

### A. Jurisdictional Basis

1. This application is brought in terms of **Rule 42(1)(a)** of the Uniform Rules of Court, which provides:

> "The court may, in addition to any other powers it may have, **mero motu** or upon the application of any party affected, rescind or vary an order or judgment erroneously sought or erroneously granted in the absence of any party affected thereby."

2. This application is also brought in terms of the Court's inherent jurisdiction to set aside orders obtained through fraud, perjury, or material non-disclosure.

3. The legal principles applicable to rescission based on fraud are well-established:

   3.1. **Fraud vitiates everything** (*fraus omnia corrumpit*);
   
   3.2. An order obtained through fraud is **void ab initio**, not merely voidable;
   
   3.3. Where fraud is proven, rescission is **mandatory**, not discretionary;
   
   3.4. The Court has an inherent duty to protect the integrity of its processes.

---

### C. Legal Consequences of Material Non-Disclosure

#### 1. Void ab initio

An order obtained through fraud, perjury, or material non-disclosure is **void ab initio** (void from inception), not merely voidable.

**Legal Authority:**

- *Schierhout v Minister of Justice* 1926 AD 99: "Fraud vitiates everything" (*fraus omnia corrumpit*);
- *Lategan v Lategan* 2001 (4) SA 1341 (SCA): Order obtained through fraud is void ab initio;
- *Giddey NO v JC Barnard and Partners* 2007 (5) SA 525 (CC): Court has inherent jurisdiction to set aside orders obtained through fraud.

**Application:**

The Part A Order was obtained through:

1. Material misrepresentation (concealing fraud report, revenue diversion, Rynette's fraud);
2. Material non-disclosure (concealing retaliatory motive, 65-day delay, prior knowledge);
3. Perjury (swearing to affidavit knowing it contained false statements and omissions).

**Conclusion:** The Part A Order is **void ab initio** and has never had legal force or effect.

---

#### 2. Mandatory Rescission

Where fraud is proven, rescission is **mandatory**, not discretionary.

**Legal Authority:**

- *Schierhout v Minister of Justice* 1926 AD 99: Court has no discretion to refuse rescission where fraud is proven;
- *Lategan v Lategan* 2001 (4) SA 1341 (SCA): Rescission is automatic consequence of fraud;
- *Giddey NO v JC Barnard and Partners* 2007 (5) SA 525 (CC): Court must set aside orders obtained through fraud to protect integrity of judicial process.

**Application:**

The Applicants have demonstrated:

1. Material misrepresentation and non-disclosure in the founding affidavit;
2. Perjury committed by the Respondent;
3. Fraud upon the Court.

**Conclusion:** The Court is **obliged** to rescind the Part A Order. There is no discretion to refuse rescission.

---

#### 3. No Need to Address Merits

Where an order is void ab initio due to fraud, there is **no need to address the merits** of the underlying dispute.

**Legal Authority:**

- *Lategan v Lategan* 2001 (4) SA 1341 (SCA): Void order has no legal effect regardless of merits;
- *Giddey NO v JC Barnard and Partners* 2007 (5) SA 525 (CC): Fraud vitiates order regardless of whether applicant would have succeeded on merits.

**Application:**

The Applicants do **not need to prove** that:

1. The Respondent's substantive allegations are false;
2. The interdict should not have been granted on the merits;
3. The Applicants would have successfully opposed the application.

**Conclusion:** The sole issue is whether the order was obtained through fraud. If proven (which it is), the order is void regardless of merits.
