## 3. LEGAL FRAMEWORK: PERJURY WITH PROVABLE KNOWLEDGE AND VOID AB INITIO

3.1 Ex parte applications require **uberrima fides** — utmost good faith. An applicant must place before the Court all material facts, including those adverse to the application. This is trite law confirmed in:

- *Schlesinger v Schlesinger* 1979 (4) SA 342 (W) at 348–349;
- *Herbstein & Van Winsen: Civil Practice of the High Court of South Africa* (5th ed) at 377;
- *Thomas Construction (Pty) Ltd (In Liquidation) v Grafton Furniture Manufacturers (Pty) Ltd* 1988 (2) SA 546 (A).

3.2 An ex parte order obtained through material non-disclosure is **void ab initio** — it never had any legal force. This Court is not merely empowered to set aside such an order; it is obligated to do so.

3.3 The standard for void ab initio is whether the undisclosed facts were **material** — that is, whether a reasonable court would have wanted to know them before granting the order.

3.4 However, this matter goes far beyond mere non-disclosure. I respectfully submit that the evidence demonstrates **perjury with provable knowledge**: the Applicant made positive false statements under oath, and at the time he made them, he possessed documentary proof that they were false. This elevates the matter from non-disclosure to active fraud on the court.

3.5 The distinction is critical:

| Category | Description | Legal Consequence |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Non-disclosure** | Failure to mention adverse facts | Order set aside; costs |
| **Material non-disclosure** | Failure to mention outcome-determinative facts | Order void ab initio; punitive costs |
| **Perjury** | Positive false statements under oath | Order void ab initio; criminal liability |
| **Perjury with provable knowledge** | False statements under oath while possessing documentary proof of their falsity | Order void ab initio; criminal liability; costs de bonis propriis; contempt |

3.6 I submit that the Applicant's conduct falls squarely within the fourth category.
