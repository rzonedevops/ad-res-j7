## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This annexure details the violations of the Protection of Personal Information Act 4 of 2013 ("POPIA") committed through the retaliatory restriction of system access on 8 July 2025, the Shopify audit trail destruction affecting customer data across 37 jurisdictions, and the exposure of the First Respondent to personal regulatory liability through no fault of her own.

---

## 2. THE 8 JULY 2025 ACCESS RESTRICTION

### 2.1 The Event

2.1.1 On or about **8 July 2025**, system access was restricted for the Second Respondent and the First Respondent. They were denied access to company information systems they were lawfully entitled to use.

2.1.2 The restriction was implemented by Rynette Farrar, who held no directorship and had no lawful authority to restrict director access.

### 2.2 The Retaliatory Context

2.2.1 The access restriction occurred after:

| Date | Event | Gap |
|------|-------|-----|
| 6 Jun 2025 | Daniel exposes fraud to Bantjies | Day 0 |
| 7 Jun 2025 | Card cancellation (retaliation) | Day 1 |
| 17 Jun 2025 | Peter demands FNB block cards | Day 11 |
| 18 Jun 2025 | FNB Legal rejects Peter's demand | Day 12 |
| **8 Jul 2025** | **System access restricted** | **Day 32** |

2.2.2 The timing — after FNB Legal rejected the Applicant's attempt to restrict Daniel's banking authority — suggests the access restriction was an alternative method of control after the banking route failed.

### 2.3 POPIA Violations

2.3.1 The access restriction constitutes the following POPIA violations:

| Section | Violation | Description |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **s5** | Right of access to personal information | Directors denied access to their own personal information in company systems |
| **s11** | Processing must be lawful | Restriction of access without lawful basis |
| **s14** | Purpose limitation | Access restricted for retaliatory purpose, not data protection |
| **s19** | Security safeguards | Access changes not authorized by data protection officer or board |

### 2.4 Companies Act Overlay

2.4.1 The Companies Act s26 grants any person with a beneficial interest the right to access company records. Directors have an enhanced right.

2.4.2 Rynette had no authority to override this statutory right. Her restriction of access constitutes both a POPIA violation and an obstruction of directors' statutory rights.

## 3. THE SHOPIFY DATA DESTRUCTION

### 3.1 Data Protection Impact

3.1.1 The Shopify audit trail deletion on 22 May 2025 destroyed personal information of customers across **37 jurisdictions**. This includes:

| Data Category | POPIA Classification | Status |
|---------------|---------------------|--------|
| Customer names and contact details | Personal information (s1) | Destroyed |
| Transaction history | Personal information (s1) | Destroyed |
| Payment information | Special personal information (s26) | Potentially destroyed |
| Communication preferences | Personal information (s1) | Destroyed |
| Delivery addresses | Personal information (s1) | Destroyed |

### 3.2 Multi-Jurisdictional Exposure

3.2.1 The Shopify platform served customers in 37 jurisdictions. Each jurisdiction has its own data protection regime:

| Jurisdiction Category | Key Legislation | Potential Penalty |
|-----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| South Africa | POPIA | R10M fine and/or imprisonment |
| European Union (GDPR) | GDPR | 4% of global turnover or EUR 20M |
| United Kingdom | UK GDPR / Data Protection Act 2018 | GBP 17.5M or 4% of global turnover |
| Other jurisdictions | Various | Variable |

3.2.2 The aggregate regulatory exposure across 37 jurisdictions is estimated at **R50,000,000+**.

### 3.3 The Responsible Person Exposure

3.3.1 The First Respondent (Jacqueline) is the designated **Responsible Person** under POPIA and multiple international data protection frameworks.

3.3.2 As Responsible Person, she bears **personal regulatory liability** for data protection failures — including the destruction of customer data she did not authorize and could not prevent.

3.3.3 The interdict compounds this exposure: by removing Jacqueline's operational authority, it prevents her from fulfilling her non-delegable regulatory duties while maintaining her personal liability.

3.3.4 This creates an impossible position:

| Obligation | Source | Status Under Interdict |
|------------|--------|----------------------|
| Protect customer data | POPIA / GDPR | Cannot fulfil — no system access |
| Respond to data subject requests | POPIA s23 | Cannot fulfil — records destroyed |
| Report data breaches | POPIA s22 | Cannot investigate — access restricted |
| Maintain data processing records | POPIA s17 | Cannot access — Sage locked out |
| **Personal liability** | **POPIA s99–107** | **Remains fully in effect** |

## 4. THE WEAPONIZATION OF DATA PROTECTION

4.1 The access restriction of 8 July 2025 was not a data protection measure. It was the weaponization of system access as an instrument of retaliation:

| Legitimate Data Protection | What Actually Happened |
|----------------------------|----------------------|
| Restricting access of unauthorized persons | Restricting access of authorized directors |
| Implemented by data protection officer or board | Implemented by an employee (Rynette) |
| Documented with lawful basis | No documentation or lawful basis |
| Proportionate to identified risk | Retaliatory response to fraud exposure |
| Applied equally | Applied selectively against fraud reporters |

## 7. LEGAL SIGNIFICANCE

7.1 The POPIA violations support the void ab initio argument because:

7.1.1 The interdict creates an impossible regulatory position for the First Respondent — maintained personal liability without operational authority;

7.1.2 The data destruction that preceded the interdict (and was concealed from the Court) exposes the companies to R50M+ in regulatory penalties;

7.1.3 A reasonable court, informed of the regulatory exposure and the Responsible Person predicament, would not have granted an interdict that compounds the harm.
