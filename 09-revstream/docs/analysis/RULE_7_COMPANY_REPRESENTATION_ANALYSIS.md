# Rule 7, Board Resolutions, and Company Representation in South African Law

**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Author:** Manus AI  
**Case Reference:** 2025-137857 (Faucitt v Faucitt)

## 1. Executive Summary

This analysis addresses the critical question: **Can a board resolution satisfy Rule 7 of the Uniform Rules by appointing a director to "speak for" a juristic entity, and how is power of attorney granted to a non-attorney?**

The answer requires distinguishing between three distinct legal concepts that are often conflated:
1. **Authorization to Litigate:** The internal corporate decision to institute or defend proceedings.
2. **Authorization to Depose:** The capacity to swear an affidavit on behalf of the company.
3. **Right of Audience (Representation):** The right to appear in court and formally represent the company.

A board resolution is the foundational document that authorizes litigation and empowers a director to sign a Rule 7 power of attorney appointing legal practitioners. However, a board resolution **cannot** grant a non-attorney director the right to appear in court on behalf of the company, nor does a director need authorization merely to depose to an affidavit.

## 2. The Chain of Authority: How Rule 7 Works for Companies

Rule 7 of the Uniform Rules of Court deals with the "Power of Attorney" to act. A common misconception is that this refers to a general commercial power of attorney. In the context of Rule 7, a "power of attorney" is specifically the document that authorizes an **admitted attorney** to act on behalf of a litigant [1].

For a juristic person (a company or close corporation), the chain of authority operates as follows:

### Step 1: The Board Resolution (Internal Governance)
Under Section 66(1) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, the business and affairs of a company must be managed by or under the direction of its board [2]. The board must pass a resolution authorizing the company to institute or defend specific legal proceedings. This resolution typically also authorizes a specific director (e.g., the Managing Director) to take all necessary steps to give effect to the resolution.

### Step 2: The Rule 7 Power of Attorney (External Mandate)
Rule 7(4) states: "Every power of attorney filed by an attorney shall be signed by or on behalf of the party giving it... provided that where a power of attorney is signed on behalf of the party giving it, proof of authority to sign on behalf of such party shall be produced to the registrar who shall note that fact on the said power" [1].

This is where the board resolution intersects with Rule 7. The company is the "party." The authorized director signs the Rule 7 power of attorney *on behalf of* the company, appointing the law firm. The board resolution serves as the "proof of authority" required by the registrar to show that the director has the right to sign the mandate.

### Step 3: Legal Representation (Right of Audience)
Once the attorney is appointed via the Rule 7 power of attorney, that attorney (and any advocate they instruct) represents the company in court.

## 3. Can a Director "Speak For" the Entity?

The phrase "speak for" must be divided into two distinct legal actions: deposing to affidavits (giving evidence) and appearing in court (legal representation).

### 3.1. Deposing to Affidavits: No Authorization Required
A director can "speak for" the company by deposing to an affidavit. Crucially, South African law is settled that a deponent to an affidavit does **not** need authorization from the company to depose to the affidavit. 

In the landmark Supreme Court of Appeal case *Ganes and Another v Telecom Namibia Ltd* (2004), Justice Streicher held:
> "The deponent to an affidavit in motion proceedings need not be authorised by the party concerned to depose to the affidavit. It is the institution of the proceedings and the prosecution thereof which must be authorised." [3]

This was recently reaffirmed by the SCA in *Masako v Masako* (2021), which clarified that a deponent is simply a witness stating facts within their personal knowledge under oath. Just as a witness giving oral evidence does not need a board resolution to testify, a deponent does not need a resolution to swear an affidavit [4]. 

Therefore, the rote inclusion of the phrase "I am duly authorized to depose to this affidavit" in founding papers is legally superfluous. What matters is whether the *institution of the proceedings* was authorized by the board.

### 3.2. Appearing in Court: The "Rule in Battle"
A director **cannot** "speak for" the company by appearing in court, arguing the case, or signing pleadings, regardless of what a board resolution says.

South African courts strictly adhere to the common law principle established in the Irish case *Battle v Irish Art Promotion Centre Ltd* (1968), known as the "Rule in Battle." This rule dictates that corporate entities may only be represented in court by legal practitioners with a formal right of audience [5].

In *Investec Securities (Pty) Ltd v Corwil Investments Holdings (Pty) Ltd* (2022), the court barred a representative from acting for a company despite him claiming he had board approval to do so, because he was not an admitted attorney or advocate [5]. 

Similarly, in the February 2026 case *Advertising Digital Services (Pty) Ltd v Standard Bank of South Africa*, the High Court struck out a notice of appeal signed by a company's sole director and shareholder. The court reaffirmed that a juristic person may not be represented by a director who is not a legal practitioner [6].

### 3.3. The "Exceptional Circumstances" Caveat
The only exception to the Rule in Battle was articulated by the SCA in *Manong & Associates (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Public Works* (2010). The court held that superior courts have a "residual discretion" arising from their inherent power to regulate their own proceedings to allow non-professional representation [7]. 

However, this is reserved for "rare and exceptional or at least unusual circumstances." A director seeking to represent a company must file a properly motivated, formal application demonstrating good cause *before* taking any steps in the proceedings. Financial constraints or the withdrawal of attorneys do not automatically qualify as exceptional circumstances [6].

## 4. Conclusion

To directly answer the question: **No, a board resolution cannot satisfy Rule 7 by appointing a non-attorney director to represent the entity in court.** 

A board resolution cannot override the common law requirement that juristic persons must be represented by admitted legal practitioners. A non-attorney cannot be granted a "power of attorney" to litigate in the Rule 7 sense.

However, a board resolution **is** the correct mechanism to:
1. Authorize the company to litigate.
2. Authorize a director to sign the Rule 7 power of attorney that appoints an actual law firm.
3. Authorize a director to instruct those attorneys.

When a director deposes to an affidavit, they are acting as a witness for the company, which requires personal knowledge of the facts, not formal corporate authorization.
