# Breaking the Catch-22: Shareholder Powers and Section 163

**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Author:** Manus AI  
**Case Reference:** 2025-137857 (Faucitt v Faucitt)

## 1. Executive Summary

The applicant has constructed a Catch-22: using a fraudulently obtained interdict to seize transactional control, defunding the respondents' legal representation, and then invoking the "Rule in Battle" to prevent the respondents from self-representing the companies. 

However, this trap has two fatal structural flaws:
1. **The Rule in Battle only applies to juristic persons (companies), not natural persons.**
2. **The interdict grants transactional control, but it does not extinguish shareholder rights or board governance powers under the Companies Act.**

This analysis outlines how the respondents can use their majority shareholding and personal capacity rights to dismantle the applicant's control structure from the inside, without requiring attorneys as a prerequisite.

## 2. The Section 163 "Personal Capacity" Bypass

The most direct way to seek subpoenas and interdicts without attorneys is to bypass the companies entirely.

Under **Section 163 of the Companies Act**, a director or shareholder may apply to court for relief from oppressive or unfairly prejudicial conduct. 
- **Crucial Distinction:** The application is brought by the director/shareholder **in their personal capacity**, not in the name of the company.
- **The Result:** Because the applicant is a natural person, the Rule in Battle does not apply. Daniel and Jacqueline can represent themselves as self-represented litigants (litigants in person).

### 2.1. Relief to Seek via Section 163
In this personal capacity application, the respondents can ask the court to order:
1. The immediate release of company funds to pay for the companies' independent legal representation.
2. A declaratory order that Rynette Farrar's access to company systems is unlawful due to her conflicts of interest.
3. Subpoenas for the communications between Rynette and Elliott Attorneys (under the crime-fraud exception).

## 3. The Section 45 "Financial Assistance" Weapon

The applicant is using company funds to pay Elliott Attorneys for his personal litigation against his co-directors. This is a massive vulnerability.

Under **Section 45 of the Companies Act**, providing financial assistance to a director requires:
1. A special resolution of the shareholders adopted within the previous two years.
2. The board must be satisfied that the terms are fair and reasonable to the company.

### 3.1. The Trap for the Applicant
The interdict is Peter's personal action. Paying his attorneys from company funds constitutes financial assistance to a director. 
- Because the respondents hold majority shareholding in 2 of the 4 entities, **no special resolution could have been passed**.
- Therefore, **every payment made to Elliott Attorneys from company funds is void *ab initio***.
- Under Section 77(3)(e)(iv), Peter is **personally liable** to repay these funds to the company.

## 4. Exercising Majority Shareholder / Director Powers

For the two entities where the respondents hold majority shareholding and/or majority board seats, the interdict does not paralyze corporate governance. The interdict grants *transactional* control (signing powers), but it does not suspend the Companies Act.

### 4.1. Restricting Rynette Farrar (Board Resolution)
Rynette is neither a director nor an employee of the specific companies. Her access is a governance issue, not a transactional one.
- The majority directors can call a board meeting and pass an ordinary resolution explicitly revoking Rynette Farrar's authorization to access any company systems, premises, or bank accounts, citing her documented identity fraud (SF10) and conflicts of interest.
- If Peter violates this board resolution by giving her access, he breaches his fiduciary duties under Section 76.

### 4.2. Ordering Payment of Legal Fees (Board Resolution)
The majority directors can pass a resolution authorizing the appointment of attorneys for the company and directing that their fees be paid.
- While Peter has transactional control (the ability to click "pay" on the bank account), he is bound by his fiduciary duty to execute lawful board resolutions.
- If the board resolves to pay the company's attorneys and Peter refuses to process the transaction, he is in direct breach of his fiduciary duties and in contempt of the board. This forms the basis for a delinquency application under Section 162.

### 4.3. The Nuclear Option: Section 71 Removal
If Peter's obstruction becomes absolute, the respondents can use their majority shareholding to remove him entirely.
- Under **Section 71(1)**, a director may be removed by an ordinary resolution (50% + 1) at a shareholders meeting.
- If the respondents hold the majority, they can convene a meeting and vote to remove Peter as a director.
- Once removed, Peter loses all directorial authority. The interdict grants powers to Peter *in his capacity as a director*. If he is no longer a director, his authority collapses.

## 5. The Step-by-Step Execution Plan

1. **The Section 45 Notice:** Send a formal notice to Peter and Elliott Attorneys stating that the use of company funds for Peter's legal fees violates Section 45 (no special resolution), that the payments are void, and demanding immediate repayment.
2. **The Board Resolutions:** Pass majority board resolutions in the 2 entities restricting Rynette's access and authorizing company legal representation.
3. **The Section 163 Application:** Daniel and Jacqueline file a Section 163 application **in person** (bypassing the Rule in Battle), attaching the Section 45 notice and the board resolutions. They ask the court to enforce the resolutions, order the release of funds for the companies' attorneys, and subpoena the Rynette-Elliott communications.
