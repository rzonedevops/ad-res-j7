# Legal Procedure for Challenging Interdicts Obtained Through Fraud

**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Author:** Manus AI  
**Case Reference:** 2025-137857 (Faucitt v Faucitt)

## 1. Executive Summary

This analysis addresses the critical procedural question: **What is the correct legal procedure to challenge an ex parte interdict obtained through fraud, particularly when the opposition argues that such a challenge violates the Uniform Rules of Court?**

The opposition's argument that challenging a fraudulent order "violates the Uniform Rules" is a fundamental misrepresentation of South African civil procedure. The Uniform Rules of Court do not shield fraudulent orders; rather, they provide the explicit statutory mechanisms for setting them aside. Furthermore, the High Court retains inherent jurisdiction under Section 165 of the Constitution to prevent abuse of its processes. 

While the legal maxim *fraus omnia corrumpit* (fraud unravels everything) applies, recent jurisprudence clarifies that a fraudulently obtained order is not automatically a common law nullity that can simply be ignored. It remains binding until formally set aside by a competent court. Therefore, the correct procedure is not to ignore the order, but to actively apply for its rescission using the mechanisms provided by the Uniform Rules and the common law.

## 2. The Procedural Framework for Rescission

When confronted with an ex parte order obtained through perjury, material non-disclosure, or fabricated evidence, the aggrieved party has three primary, often overlapping, procedural avenues to set the order aside.

### 2.1. Rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules of Court

Rule 42(1)(a) is the primary statutory mechanism for rescinding an order obtained *ex parte*. The rule states that a court may rescind or vary "an order or judgment erroneously sought or erroneously granted in the absence of any party affected thereby" [1].

An order is "erroneously sought" when the applicant relies on perjured evidence or fails to disclose material facts. In *ex parte* applications, the applicant owes a duty of utmost good faith (*uberrima fides*) to the court. They must disclose all material facts that might influence the court's decision, even those adverse to their case. 

In the context of Case 2025-137857, the applicant's failure to disclose the "SOLE authority" banking mandate, the prior confirmation of that authority by FNB Legal, and the retaliatory nature of the application constitutes a gross violation of the *uberrima fides* rule. This renders the order "erroneously sought" within the meaning of Rule 42(1)(a), providing a direct, rule-compliant pathway for rescission.

### 2.2. Rule 6(12)(c) Reconsideration

For orders granted under the rules of urgency, Rule 6(12)(c) provides a specific and powerful remedy: "A person against whom an order was granted in such person's absence in an urgent application may by notice, set down the matter for reconsideration of the order" [2].

This is not a discretionary remedy but a right afforded to the absent party. When a matter is set down for reconsideration under this sub-rule, the court considers the matter *de novo* (anew), taking into account the answering affidavits and the evidence of fraud or non-disclosure that was concealed from the judge who granted the initial order.

### 2.3. Common Law Rescission for Fraud

Alongside the Uniform Rules, the High Court retains its inherent common law jurisdiction to set aside judgments obtained by fraud. The foundational principle is *fraus omnia corrumpit*—fraud unravels everything [3]. 

To succeed under the common law, the applicant for rescission must prove:
1. That the successful litigant was a party to the fraud.
2. That the evidence was in fact incorrect (perjury or fabrication).
3. That it was made fraudulently and with intent to mislead.
4. That it diverged to such an extent from the true facts that the court would, if the true facts had been placed before it, have given a judgment other than that which it was induced by the false evidence to give.

The `provable-foreknowledge` framework applied to the evidence in Case 2025-137857 definitively establishes these elements, proving that the applicant had prior knowledge that his sworn statements regarding "unauthorized" transactions were false.

## 3. The "Nullity" Fallacy and the Necessity of Procedure

A critical distinction must be made between an order that is *void ab initio* (void from the beginning) due to fraud, and the procedural requirement to have it declared as such.

In the recent case of *Van Dyk v Rhodes* (2024), the Gauteng High Court clarified that the common law doctrine of nullity does not allow a party to simply ignore a court order [4]. Court orders derive their validity from Section 165(5) of the Constitution. The court held: "A court order can no longer be ignored or rescinded merely upon proof that it would have been regarded as a common law nullity. The ordinary principles of rescission or appeal will always apply to court orders wrongly granted, no matter what error led to their issuance" [4].

Therefore, while the fraud renders the foundation of the interdict void, the order itself remains technically in force until a formal application (under Rule 42 or the common law) is brought to set it aside. The opposition's argument that challenging the order violates the rules is false; failing to formally challenge it and simply ignoring it would be the procedural error.

## 4. Fraud as a Defense to Contempt Proceedings

If the perpetrators attempt to enforce the fraudulently obtained interdict through contempt of court proceedings, the fraud serves as a complete defense.

In the landmark Supreme Court of Appeal case *Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd*, the court established that civil contempt requires the non-compliance to be both "wilful and mala fide" (in bad faith) [5]. The court explicitly noted that an honest belief that non-compliance is justified is incompatible with the intent required for contempt.

If a respondent refuses to comply with an order because they possess documentary evidence proving the order was obtained through perjury and fraud, their non-compliance is *bona fide*. They are not deliberately violating the court's dignity; they are refusing to participate in the weaponization of the court's processes. 

When faced with a contempt application based on a fraudulent order, the correct procedure is to file an answering affidavit raising the fraud as a defense (negating *mala fides*), coupled with a counter-application under Rule 6(7) for the rescission of the original order under Rule 42(1)(a).

## 5. Conclusion and Strategic Directive

The opposition's assertion that submitting evidence of fraud violates the Uniform Rules is a tactical misdirection designed to suppress the truth. The Uniform Rules are the very instruments designed to rectify such abuses.

**The correct procedural response is:**
1. **Do not ignore the order:** Acknowledge its existence but immediately attack its validity.
2. **File a Rescission Application:** Bring a formal application under Rule 42(1)(a) read with the common law, seeking to set aside the ex parte order on the grounds that it was erroneously sought through material non-disclosure and perjury.
3. **Invoke Rule 6(12)(c):** If the original order was urgent, simultaneously file a notice for reconsideration.
4. **Counter-Attack Contempt:** If facing contempt proceedings, use the evidence of fraud to demonstrate *bona fides* and file a counter-application for rescission.

By strictly adhering to these procedures, the aggrieved party utilizes the Uniform Rules to dismantle the fraudulent interdict, turning the opposition's procedural arguments against them.
