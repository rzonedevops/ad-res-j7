# The "Catch-22" Interdict: Weaponizing Court Orders to Deny Legal Representation

**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Author:** Manus AI  
**Case Reference:** 2025-137857 (Faucitt v Faucitt)

## 1. Executive Summary

This analysis addresses a severe abuse of process: a scenario where an applicant obtains a fraudulent interdict granting them sole transactional control of corporate entities, uses company funds to pay their own legal team, but refuses to process transactions for the respondent directors' legal representation. 

This creates a perfect "Catch-22":
1. The respondents need attorneys to challenge the fraudulent interdict.
2. The respondents cannot hire attorneys because the applicant controls the funds via the interdict.
3. If the respondents try to represent the company themselves, the applicant argues this violates the "Rule in Battle" (requiring admitted attorneys).
4. Therefore, the fraudulent order is weaponized to prevent its own rescission.

This conduct is not merely a procedural dispute; it is a fundamental violation of Section 34 of the Constitution, a breach of fiduciary duty, and a textbook abuse of process. This document outlines the legal implications and the specific procedural avenues available to break this deadlock.

## 2. Legal Implications of the Applicant's Conduct

### 2.1. Violation of Section 34 of the Constitution
Section 34 of the Constitution guarantees that "Everyone has the right to have any dispute that can be resolved by the application of law decided in a fair public hearing before a court" [1]. 

This is an *enabling* right. When a litigant uses a court order to deliberately starve their opponent of the means to obtain legal representation—while funding their own representation from the very same corporate pool—they are actively subverting the Constitution. The court's processes are being used to deny access to the court.

### 2.2. Abuse of Process
South African courts have inherent jurisdiction to prevent the abuse of their processes. As noted in recent legal commentary, "The abuse of South Africa's legal system by scrupulous defendants is not merely a technical issue; it is a moral and ethical failure that undermines justice" [2]. 

Creating a scenario where an opponent is legally required to have an attorney (due to the Rule in Battle for companies) but financially prevented from hiring one by the applicant's unilateral control of shared funds constitutes a gross abuse of process.

### 2.3. Breach of Fiduciary Duties
Under Section 76 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, a director must act in good faith, for a proper purpose, and in the best interests of the company [3]. 

By using company funds to finance his own legal representation in a dispute between directors, while simultaneously refusing to release company funds for the co-directors' legal representation or the entities' independent representation, the applicant is:
- Using his position for personal gain (s76(2)(a)).
- Failing to act in the best interests of the company (s76(3)(b)), as the company is left unrepresented or unevenly represented.
- Acting oppressively toward his co-directors.

## 3. Procedural Avenues to Break the Deadlock

The respondents are not without remedy. The law provides several mechanisms to pierce this Catch-22.

### Avenue 1: The Rule 43 Analogy (Urgent Interlocutory Application)
In divorce proceedings, Rule 43 (High Court) and Rule 58 (Magistrates' Court) exist precisely for this power imbalance: when one spouse controls the finances and uses that control to deny the other access to legal representation, the court can order a contribution to legal costs [4]. The underlying principle is equality of arms.

While Rule 43 is specific to matrimonial matters, the *principle* applies to corporate disputes via the court's inherent jurisdiction. The respondents can bring an urgent interlocutory application seeking an order directing the applicant to release a specified amount of company funds to the respondents' attorneys in trust, to ensure equality of arms in the main dispute.

### Avenue 2: Section 163 of the Companies Act (Oppressive Conduct)
Section 163 permits a director to apply to court for relief if any act by the company or a related person is "oppressive or unfairly prejudicial" [5]. 

The deliberate starvation of legal funds by a co-director wielding sole transactional control is the definition of oppressive conduct. Under Section 163(2), the court has a "wide discretion to craft an order appropriately redressing any impugned act or omission" [6]. 

**Action:** The respondents (in their personal capacities as directors) can launch a Section 163 application seeking an order that the company must fund their legal representation equally to the applicant's, or that the applicant must repay the company funds used for his personal legal fees.

### Avenue 3: Application for "Exceptional Circumstances" (The Manong Exception)
As established in *Manong & Associates (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Public Works* (2010), courts have a "residual discretion" to allow a non-professional director to represent a company in "rare and exceptional or at least unusual circumstances" [7].

While financial constraints alone do not usually qualify, the *deliberate, bad-faith starvation of funds by the opposing party using a court order* is highly exceptional. 

**Action:** A respondent director can file a formal, motivated application for leave to represent the company, explicitly citing the applicant's Catch-22 tactic as the "exceptional circumstance." If the court denies this, it must logically order the release of funds to hire an attorney, as it cannot constitutionally deny both.

### Avenue 4: Rule 6(12)(c) Reconsideration
If the original interdict was granted *ex parte* or on an urgent basis in the respondents' absence, Rule 6(12)(c) allows the respondents to set the matter down for reconsideration as a matter of right [8]. 

**Action:** The respondents can file a notice of reconsideration. At this hearing, the court reconsiders the matter *de novo* (from the beginning). The respondents can present the FNB Legal letter, the SOLE authority mandate, and the provable foreknowledge register to show the original order was obtained through fraud, and simultaneously raise the issue of the applicant's abuse of the transactional control.

## 4. Strategic Recommendation

The most aggressive and effective counter-maneuver is a **two-pronged attack**:

1. **File a Section 163 Application** (in the directors' personal capacities, bypassing the Rule in Battle) citing the applicant's refusal to fund their legal representation as oppressive conduct and a breach of fiduciary duty.
2. **Simultaneously file an Urgent Interlocutory Application** in the main matter, asking the court to either:
   - (a) Order the immediate release of company funds to the respondents' attorneys to ensure equality of arms (the Rule 43 principle); OR
   - (b) Grant leave under the *Manong* exception for the directors to represent the entities, given the applicant's deliberate obstruction of their ability to hire counsel.

By presenting these options to the judge, you expose the applicant's strategy not as a legitimate legal defense, but as a bad-faith abuse of process designed to defeat the ends of justice.
