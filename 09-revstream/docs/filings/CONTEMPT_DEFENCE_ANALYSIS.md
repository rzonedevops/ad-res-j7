## 1. The Legal Framework: *Fakie NO v CCII Systems*

The authoritative test for civil contempt of court in South African law was established by the Supreme Court of Appeal in *Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd* [1]. The court held that an applicant must prove four elements to succeed in a contempt application:

1. The existence of a clear, precise, and enforceable court order.
2. Knowledge of the order by the respondent.
3. Non-compliance with the order (contravention).
4. Wilfulness and *mala fides* (bad faith) on the part of the respondent.

The *Fakie* judgment established a critical procedural safeguard regarding the burden of proof. Because civil contempt proceedings invoke a criminal sanction (committal to prison), the applicant must prove all elements **beyond a reasonable doubt** [1]. 

Once the applicant proves the first three elements (the order, knowledge, and non-compliance), a presumption arises that the non-compliance was wilful and *mala fide*. The evidentiary burden then shifts to the respondent to advance evidence that establishes a reasonable doubt as to whether the non-compliance was wilful and *mala fide* [1]. As Cameron JA noted, "A deliberate disregard is not enough, since the non-complier may genuinely, albeit mistakenly, believe him- or herself entitled to act in the way claimed to constitute the contempt" [1].

## 2. Analysis of Element 3: Contravention (Non-Compliance)

The applicant's contempt application is predicated on three specific factual allegations of non-compliance. The first respondent's answering affidavit, supported by three independent confirmatory affidavits, systematically dismantles each allegation, demonstrating that the applicant cannot prove Element 3 beyond a reasonable doubt.

### 2.1 Allegation 1: Obstruction of Stock Take (4 February 2026)

The applicant alleges that the first respondent prevented Oliver Mphande from conducting a stock take and removing products on 4 February 2026. 

The first respondent categorically denies this allegation. The answering affidavit demonstrates that a RégimA Stockist Product Order Form was sent to Oliver, who collected the requested products, packaged them, and loaded them onto the delivery vehicle in accordance with established practice. This is corroborated by contemporaneous documentary evidence: a photograph taken by the security officer showing the stock collection (Annexure "JF5") and the completed order form recording the removed products (Annexure "JF6").

### 2.2 Allegation 2: Obstruction of Stock Take (4-5 March 2026)

The applicant alleges further obstruction on 4 and 5 March 2026. The evidentiary record demonstrates the precise opposite.

On 4 March 2026, an order form was delivered to Oliver, who committed to having the large order ready by Friday, 6 March 2026. On 5 March, the applicant insisted on an early partial delivery and personally removed 312 units. On 6 March at 09:15, the full remaining order was loaded onto the delivery truck, which the applicant personally drove to the warehouse.

This timeline is fatal to the applicant's case for two reasons. First, the applicant deposed to his founding affidavit under oath while the products were actively being loaded onto the delivery truck. Second, the contempt application was served at 15:20 on 6 March—six hours *after* the full order had been completed and collected by the applicant himself.

This rebuttal is supported by two critical confirmatory affidavits from first-hand witnesses:
*   **Oliver Mphande (Annexure "JF8"):** Confirms that the first respondent did not obstruct the stock collection and that the timeline of delivery was agreed upon.
*   **Edgar Mphande (Annexure "JF10"):** Confirms witnessing the applicant arriving in the delivery truck on 6 March to collect the completed order.

### 2.3 Allegation 3: Disruption of Training (23-24 February 2026)

The applicant alleges that the first respondent disrupted a training session. The answering affidavit clarifies the legal and operational context of these sessions. The training programmes are the personal intellectual property of the first respondent, operated under RégimA Zone Academy Ltd (UK) and RégimA Zone Academy (Pty) Ltd (ZA)—entities that are not subject to the interdict.

The first respondent attended the training in her established supervisory capacity, as she is the only individual authorised to sign the training certificates. Her dismissal of administrative staff (Rynette Farrar and Gayane Williams) after their refreshment and setup duties were completed was a legitimate exercise of her supervisory role, not a disruption of the training itself.

This is corroborated by the confirmatory affidavit of **Cherie Smith (Annexure "JF11")**, the training assistant, who confirms that the training was not disrupted.

### 2.4 Conclusion on Element 3

The applicant's version of events is based entirely on hearsay, as he was not present at the warehouse on 4 February or at the training on 23-24 February. He has provided zero confirmatory affidavits from persons with direct knowledge. In contrast, the first respondent has provided three confirmatory affidavits from independent, first-hand witnesses (Oliver, Edgar, and Smith) that directly contradict the applicant's claims. Consequently, the applicant cannot prove non-compliance beyond a reasonable doubt, and the contempt application must fail at the third *Fakie* element.

## 3. Analysis of Element 4: Wilfulness and *Mala Fides*

Even if the applicant could somehow establish non-compliance (which is denied), the contempt application would still fail on the fourth element. The first respondent has advanced overwhelming evidence to establish a reasonable doubt regarding wilfulness and *mala fides*.

The *Fakie* judgment establishes that an honest belief that conduct is justified precludes a finding of *mala fides* [1]. The first respondent's conduct regarding the training sessions was based on her undisputed status as the creator and sole authorised signatory of the training programmes, which fall outside the interdicted corporate entities. Her actions were a bona fide exercise of her personal intellectual property rights.

Furthermore, the evidence demonstrates that the first respondent facilitated compliance with the stock orders within the operational timeframes committed to by the warehouse staff. There is no evidence of deliberate, bad-faith defiance of the court's authority.

## 4. The Void *Ab Initio* Overlay: *Fraus Omnia Corrumpit*

Beyond the failure to meet the *Fakie* requirements, the contempt application suffers from a fundamental jurisdictional defect: the underlying ex parte interdict of 19 August 2025 was obtained through fraud on the court.

South African law recognises the principle of *fraus omnia corrumpit*—fraud vitiates everything it touches. As established in cases such as *Giddey NO v JC Barnard and Partners* [2], an order obtained through fraud or material non-disclosure is void *ab initio* (void from the beginning). A void order is a legal nullity and cannot create enforceable obligations. Consequently, a void order cannot serve as the foundational element (Element 1 of the *Fakie* test) for a contempt application.

The answering affidavit details five pillars of invalidity demonstrating that the interdict was obtained through perjury with provable foreknowledge and material non-disclosure:
1.  **The Sage Predicate Crime:** The applicant swore a forged affidavit on 8 July 2024 to seize control of the Sage accounting system, severing the API chain. He then committed perjury on 13 August 2025 by blaming the second respondent for the resulting lack of invoices.
2.  **Material Non-Disclosure:** The applicant concealed the Sage forgery, the API destruction, and the extraction of R10.6 million from the entities' accounts within days of obtaining the interdict.
3.  **Supporting Affidavit Fraud:** The "independent" accountant, D.J. Bantjies, swore a supporting affidavit concealing his prior knowledge of the infrastructure sabotage and his irreconcilable conflicts of interest regarding the Faucitt Family Trust.

Because the underlying order is void *ab initio*, the contempt application is legally incompetent.

## 5. Red-Team Critique and Vulnerability Assessment

While the contempt defence is robust, a red-team critique identifies potential vulnerabilities that must be managed:

### Vulnerability 1: The "Self-Help" Argument (*Oudekraal* Principle)
The applicant may argue that even if the order was obtained by fraud, it remains binding until formally set aside by a competent court (the *Oudekraal* principle), and that the first respondent cannot rely on its alleged invalidity to justify non-compliance.
*   **Rebuttal Strategy:** The primary defence must remain the factual denial of non-compliance (Element 3), supported by the confirmatory affidavits. The void *ab initio* argument should be positioned as a secondary, compounding factor demonstrating the applicant's *mala fides* and abuse of process, rather than an admission of non-compliance justified by the order's invalidity. The conditional counter-application to set aside the interdict (filed 12 March 2026) cures the procedural requirement to formally challenge the order.

### Vulnerability 2: Witness Intimidation and Credibility Attacks
The applicant's agents have demonstrated a willingness to target witnesses, as evidenced by the arrest of Oliver Mphande and the protection order served on the first respondent. The applicant may attempt to attack the credibility of the confirmatory affidavits by citing these criminal complaints.
*   **Rebuttal Strategy:** This pattern of witness intimidation must be aggressively highlighted to the court as evidence of the applicant's *mala fides* and an attempt to defeat the ends of justice. The fact that Oliver's confirmatory affidavit was secured despite his arrest strengthens its evidentiary weight. The retaliatory nature of the perjury complaint (Case No. 222/11/2025) must be exposed as a violation of section 36 of the Criminal Procedure Act.

### Vulnerability 3: The "Partial Compliance" Argument
The applicant may argue that the delivery of 312 units on 5 March 2026 was insufficient and constituted partial non-compliance with the order form of 4 March.
*   **Rebuttal Strategy:** The answering affidavit clearly establishes that the 312 units were what the applicant *chose* to take early, before the committed delivery date of 6 March. The full order was completed and loaded by 09:15 on 6 March, and the applicant personally drove the delivery truck. The timeline proves that compliance was achieved within the operational timeframes agreed upon, negating any claim of wilful obstruction.

## 6. Conclusion

The applicant's contempt application is fatally defective. It fails the *Fakie* test on Element 3, as the factual allegations of non-compliance are comprehensively rebutted by contemporaneous documentary evidence and three independent confirmatory affidavits. It fails on Element 4, as there is no evidence of wilful or *mala fide* defiance. Finally, the application is built upon an ex parte interdict that is void *ab initio* due to established fraud and perjury. The application constitutes an abuse of process and should be dismissed with punitive costs.
