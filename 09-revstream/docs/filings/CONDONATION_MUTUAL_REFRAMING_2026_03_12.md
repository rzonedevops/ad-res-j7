## Executive Summary

The current litigation has devolved into a procedural skirmish, with the Applicant (Peter Faucitt) using Rule 7(1) and Rule 30/30A notices to block the court from considering substantive evidence of perjury and fraud. However, the Applicant's own procedural notices contain glaring temporal impossibilities (dated 03 February 2026, but critiquing a document served on 19 February 2026). 

This document outlines a strategic legal argument for **mutual condonation**, urging the court to look past the procedural defects of *both* parties and focus on the substantive evidence. The argument reframes the proceedings around the true purpose of an *ex parte* application: to protect the entities, not to weaponize the court against an opponent.

## 1. The Procedural Stalemate

Both parties have committed procedural errors that technically render their current filings irregular under the Uniform Rules of Court.

### The Respondents' Defects
The "Entity Answering Affidavit" served on 19 February 2026 was procedurally defective. It was drafted in the name of juristic entities (who cannot swear an oath), lacked attached board resolutions demonstrating authority, improperly combined a rescission application with a contempt opposition, and was not properly commissioned.

### The Applicant's Defects
The Applicant's Rule 7(1) and Rule 30/30A notices, served on 03 March 2026, are both explicitly dated and signed **"03 DAY OF FEBRUARY 2026"**. This creates a temporal impossibility, as they critique a document that did not exist until 16 days later. This clerical error technically invalidates the 10-day deadline imposed by the Rule 30/30A notice, as the deadline would have expired before the triggering document was even served.

## 2. The Legal Argument for Mutual Condonation

Under Rule 27(3) of the Uniform Rules of Court, the court has wide discretion to condone any non-compliance with the rules upon good cause shown. The argument for condonation rests on three pillars:

### Pillar 1: Substance Over Form
The Constitutional Court in *Afrocentrics Projects and Services (Pty) Ltd v SITA* [2023] ZACC 2 affirmed that Rule 30 applies to irregularities of form, not matters of substance. Furthermore, as articulated in *Louw v Grobler*, "dogmatically rigid adherence to the uniform court rules is as distasteful as their flagrant disregard or violation." 

When both parties have committed errors of form, the court should not allow a "gotcha" procedural victory to suppress substantive evidence of fraud. The dating discrepancy on the Applicant's notices demonstrates that procedural imperfections are a normal incident of litigation, even for experienced attorneys.

### Pillar 2: Absence of Prejudice
As established in *Bloem v NWK Limited* [2024] ZANWHC 83, procedural steps may be irregular but need not be set aside if they cause no prejudice in the further conduct of proceedings. The Applicant suffers no prejudice by the court considering the substantive evidence of the *void ab initio* defense, provided the Respondents cure the formal defects (e.g., by providing the missing board resolutions and properly commissioning the affidavits).

### Pillar 3: The Duty of the Court in Cases of Fraud
The most compelling reason for condonation is the nature of the allegations. The Respondents have presented documentary evidence (including FNB mandates and correspondence) suggesting the *ex parte* interdict was obtained through perjury and material non-disclosure. 

Under the doctrine of *fraus omnia corrumpit* (fraud unravels all), a court has an inherent jurisdiction and a positive duty to investigate allegations that its own processes have been abused. A court cannot allow procedural rules to act as a shield for perjury.

## 3. Reframing the Ex Parte Application

The Applicant's procedural aggression attempts to frame this matter as a standard adversarial dispute where rules must be strictly enforced to punish the opponent. This fundamentally mischaracterizes the nature of the proceedings.

### The True Purpose of Ex Parte Relief
An *ex parte* application is an extraordinary remedy granted without notice to the affected party. Its sole legitimate purpose is to protect urgent interests (in this case, the assets of the corporate entities) from imminent harm. It is **not** a weapon to be used to attack an opponent or seize control of a company for personal gain.

### The Requirement of Uberrima Fides
Because the court hears only one side in an *ex parte* application, the law imposes a duty of *uberrima fides* (utmost good faith) on the applicant. They must disclose all material facts, even those adverse to their case. 

The evidence now before the court (the FNB SOLE mandate, the FNB Legal letter, the context of the R500,000 reimbursement) strongly indicates this duty was breached. If the Applicant misled the court to obtain the interdict, the interdict is *void ab initio*.

### Protecting the Entities
The court's primary concern must be the wellbeing of the four corporate entities (RWD, RST, VVA, SLG). If the substantive evidence shows that the Applicant—who obtained the interdict ostensibly to protect the entities—is actually the party extracting funds (R10.9 million extracted within 8 days of the order), the court must adapt the interdict to protect the entities from the true threat.

An urgent application is about urgency and protection, not about securing a tactical advantage through procedural maneuvering.

## 4. Proposed Resolution

To resolve the procedural stalemate and serve the interests of justice, the Respondents propose the following practical path forward:

1. **Mutual Condonation:** The court condones the Applicant's impossible dating of the Rule 30/30A notices, and simultaneously condones the Respondents' formal defects in the Entity Answering Affidavit.
2. **Opportunity to Cure:** The Respondents are granted leave to file a properly commissioned Founding Affidavit for Rescission, complete with the requisite board resolutions, placing the substantive evidence properly before the court.
3. **Focus on Substance:** The court proceeds to hear the substantive arguments regarding whether the 19 August 2025 interdict was obtained through material non-disclosure and perjury, and whether it should be set aside as *void ab initio*.

By adopting this approach, the court fulfills its duty to protect its own integrity and the wellbeing of the corporate entities, rather than allowing the litigation to be derailed by mutual procedural technicalities.
