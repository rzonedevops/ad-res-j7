# Legal Precedent Authority: Case 2025-137857

This document compiles the authoritative South African legal precedents governing the core legal issues in Case 2025-137857, specifically addressing void ab initio orders, ex parte non-disclosure, perjury, contempt of court, director delinquency, and racketeering.

## 1. Void Ab Initio and Ex Parte Non-Disclosure

The foundational principle governing ex parte applications in South African law is the duty of utmost good faith (*uberrima fides*). An applicant approaching the court without notice to the respondent must disclose all material facts that might influence the court's decision.

In the seminal case of **Spur Steak Ranches Ltd v Saddles Steak Ranch, Claremont** [1], the Cape Provincial Division established that an applicant who approaches the court on an ex parte basis is under a duty to make full and frank disclosure of all material facts. If the applicant fails to do so, the court may discharge the order even if the applicant was otherwise entitled to the relief sought. This principle was recently reaffirmed in **Sanlam Life Insurance Ltd v Nellwyn CC** [2], where the South Gauteng High Court held that the failure to disclose material communications and ongoing settlement negotiations to the urgent court warrants reconsideration of the order under Uniform Rule 6(12)(c).

Furthermore, the leading textbook authority, **Herbstein and Van Winsen** [3], states unequivocally that non-disclosure in an ex parte application is in law tantamount to fraudulent conduct. In the context of Case 2025-137857, Peter Faucitt's failure to disclose the FNB sole mandate letter, the card sabotage, and the R500K reimbursement context constitutes a textbook violation of the *Spur Steak Ranches* rule, rendering the resulting interdict void ab initio.

## 2. Fraud on the Court and Common Law Rescission

Under South African common law, an order obtained by fraud is a nullity. The maxim *fraus omnia corrumpit* (fraud vitiates everything) applies absolutely.

In **Chetty v Law Society, Transvaal** [4], the Appellate Division held that an applicant for rescission under common law must show "sufficient cause." However, where the order was obtained by fraud, the fraud itself constitutes sufficient cause. A judgment obtained by fraud is a nullity and can be set aside independently of the requirements of Uniform Rule 42. This was recently confirmed in **Land and Agricultural Development Bank v Nagel** [5], where the North West High Court reiterated that "no court will give effect to a fraud."

## 3. Contempt of Court

The test for civil contempt of court was definitively established by the Supreme Court of Appeal in **Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd** [6].

The SCA held that disobedience of a civil order only constitutes contempt if the breach was committed "deliberately and mala fide." Crucially, the court noted that a deliberate disregard is not enough:

> "A deliberate disregard is not enough, since the non-complier may genuinely, albeit mistakenly, believe him- or herself entitled to act in the way claimed to constitute the contempt. In such a case good faith avoids the infraction. Honest belief that non-compliance is justified or proper is incompatible with that intent." [6]

In the present case, the contempt application against Jacqueline fails the *Fakie* test on two grounds: first, the underlying order is void ab initio (and one cannot be in contempt of a nullity); second, her honest belief that the order was obtained through fraud constitutes good faith, negating the *mala fide* requirement.

## 4. Perjury and False Affidavits

Perjury in South Africa exists both as a common law offence and a statutory offence.

Common law perjury is defined as the deliberate, unlawful act of providing false testimony while under oath during legal proceedings. Statutory perjury, governed by Section 9 of the **Justices of the Peace and Commissioners of Oaths Act 16 of 1963**, was analyzed in **S v Ncamane** [7]. The Free State High Court confirmed that statutory perjury is an independent substantive offence with four elements: (i) a false statement; (ii) in an affidavit, affirmation or attested declaration; (iii) made before a person competent to administer an oath; and (iv) knowing it to be false.

Bantjies' J417 declaration of "Independent Trustee" status to the Master of the High Court satisfies all four elements, as it was a sworn declaration made before a commissioner of oaths, and he knew it was false given his simultaneous employment as CFO of The George Group.

## 5. Director Delinquency and Personal Liability

The Companies Act 71 of 2008 provides stringent remedies for director misconduct.

Under **Section 162(5)(c)**, a court *must* declare a director delinquent if they acted in a manner that amounted to gross negligence, wilful misconduct, or breach of trust. As noted in recent legal analysis [8], the court has no discretion once the threshold is met. In **Gihwala v Grancy Property Ltd** [9], the SCA confirmed that wilful obstruction of a company's obligations constitutes wilful misconduct and breach of fiduciary duty.

Furthermore, **Section 77(2)** holds directors personally liable for breaches of fiduciary duties, while **Section 77(3)(b)** imposes liability for acquiescing in the carrying on of the company's business recklessly, with gross negligence, or with intent to defraud. Peter Faucitt's systematic diversion of revenue and creation of parallel entities trigger both mandatory delinquency and personal liability.

## 6. The Plascon-Evans Rule

In motion proceedings where facts are disputed, the **Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd** [10] rule applies. The court must decide the matter on the respondent's version of facts, except where the respondent's version is so far-fetched or clearly untenable that it can be rejected on the papers. Given the documentary evidence of the FNB sole mandate and revenue diversion, Peter's version of events is untenable and stands to be rejected under this rule.
