## Description

On 10 and 12 January 2026, Elliott Attorneys (for Peter) and Pottas Attorneys (for Jacqueline) discussed the stock-blocking incident telephonically.

**PF15 - Elliott Attorneys Demand (12 January 2026):**
Elliott Attorneys addressed a letter to Pottas Attorneys recording that Jacqueline was preventing the removal of stock from storage, thereby preventing provision to the businesses' clients. The letter explicitly stated that her conduct constituted contempt of court and demanded stock release by 14:00 on the same day.

**PF16 - Pottas Attorneys Response (12 January 2026, 15:04):**
Pottas Attorneys replied the same day. Critically, the respondent's conduct was **never denied**. Instead, a vague attempt was made to defend the conduct by stating:
1. Jacqueline was "extremely concerned that stock will be stolen and/or taken and not accounted for"
2. Jacqueline now required "adequate notice" for stock collection
3. Suggested an independent third party for stock take

## Legal Significance

The response from Pottas Attorneys is significant because:
1. **The contempt was never denied** - constituting an effective admission
2. The demand for "adequate notice" was unprecedented - this was the first time such a requirement was imposed, destroying any claim of reasonableness
3. The concern about stock being "stolen" is contradicted by the established norm of stock removal for business operations
4. Per the founding affidavit (para 8.6): "the respondent thereby effectively admitted her contempt"
