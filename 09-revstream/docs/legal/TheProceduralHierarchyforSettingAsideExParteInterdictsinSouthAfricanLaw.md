# The Procedural Hierarchy for Setting Aside Ex Parte Interdicts in South African Law

By Manus AI

The granting of an *ex parte* interdict is a drastic departure from the fundamental legal principle of *audi alteram partem* (let the other side be heard). Because the respondent is deprived of the opportunity to present their case before the order is made, the law imposes strict duties on the applicant and provides robust mechanisms for the respondent to challenge the order. 

This document provides a comprehensive analysis of the five-tier procedural hierarchy available to a respondent seeking to set aside an *ex parte* interdict, ranging from the primary mechanism of reconsideration to the residual power of inherent jurisdiction.

## 1. The Foundational Principle: Uberrima Fides and the Duty of Disclosure

Before examining the procedural remedies, it is necessary to understand the substantive basis upon which *ex parte* orders are most frequently challenged: material non-disclosure.

In *ex parte* applications, the applicant bears a duty of utmost good faith (*uberrima fides*). They must place all material facts before the court, including those that might be detrimental to their case. The leading authority on this duty is the seminal judgment in *Schlesinger v Schlesinger* [1], where Le Roux J articulated the following principles:

> "(1) in ex parte applications all material facts must be disclosed which might influence a Court in coming to a decision;
> (2) the non-disclosure or suppression of facts need not be wilful or mala fide to incur the penalty of rescission; and
> (3) the Court, apprised of the true facts, has a discretion to set aside the former order or to preserve it."

If an applicant fails to disclose material facts—whether intentionally, negligently, or innocently—the court has the discretion to set aside the order. When the non-disclosure rises to the level of deliberate perjury with provable foreknowledge, the consequences escalate dramatically, as fraud vitiates the entire proceeding (*fraus omnia corrumpit*) [2].

## 2. The Five-Tier Procedural Hierarchy

When a respondent becomes aware of an *ex parte* interdict granted against them, they must select the correct procedural mechanism to challenge it. The choice depends on the nature of the order (interim vs. final), the urgency of the matter, and the grounds for the challenge.

### Tier 1: Rule 6(12)(c) Reconsideration (The Primary Remedy)

Uniform Rule 6(12)(c) provides that any person against whom an order was granted *ex parte* in an urgent application may set the matter down for reconsideration upon no less than 24 hours' notice [3]. This is the primary and most powerful mechanism for challenging an *ex parte* urgent interdict.

The nature of a Rule 6(12)(c) reconsideration is a *de novo* hearing. The court considers the matter afresh, taking into account both the original founding papers and any new affidavits filed by the respondent. The court is not limited to reviewing whether the original judge was correct on the papers before them; rather, it determines whether the order should have been granted based on the complete picture now presented [4].

Crucially, Rule 6(12)(c) applies to both interim and final orders granted on an urgent *ex parte* basis [5]. The court exercises the broadest possible discretion, as the rule explicitly states that the court may "make any order it deems fit." The respondent does not need to show "good cause" or a *bona fide* defence; they merely need to demonstrate that, upon a consideration of all the facts, the order is not justified.

### Tier 2: Rule 6(8) / Rule 8 Anticipation of the Return Day

Uniform Rule 6(8) allows any person against whom an order is granted *ex parte* to anticipate the return day upon delivery of not less than 24 hours' notice [6]. This mechanism is specifically designed for interim orders (rules *nisi*) that have a scheduled return date.

The purpose of anticipation is to allow the respondent to bring the matter before the court sooner than the return date set by the original judge. Like Rule 6(12)(c), it requires 24 hours' notice. However, a critical distinction exists between the two rules: anticipation applies **only** to interim orders with a return day. 

As Lagrange J clarified in *SA Post Office v CCMA* [5], if the original order was final in nature (meaning there is no return day), an application to anticipate under Rule 8 (the Labour Court equivalent) is misconceived. In such cases, the respondent must rely on Rule 6(12)(c) or Rule 42. While practitioners often use Rule 6(12)(c) and Rule 6(8) interchangeably or in the alternative (as seen in *Nku v Ramokoka* [7]), understanding the technical distinction is essential for proper pleading.

### Tier 3: Rule 42(1)(a) Rescission

Uniform Rule 42(1)(a) empowers a court to rescind or vary an order or judgment "erroneously sought or erroneously granted in the absence of any party affected thereby" [8]. 

In the context of *ex parte* interdicts, Rule 42(1)(a) is typically invoked when the respondent can demonstrate that a procedural error occurred or that the applicant failed to disclose material facts. A material non-disclosure renders the order "erroneously sought" because the original judge was deprived of the full facts necessary to make a proper determination [9].

Unlike the common law requirements for rescission, an applicant under Rule 42(1)(a) does not need to show "good cause" or a *bona fide* defence [10]. Once it is established that the order was erroneously sought or granted, the court generally has no discretion but to rescind it. However, Rule 42(1)(a) is narrower in scope than Rule 6(12)(c) because the respondent must fit their challenge squarely within the "erroneously sought/granted" framework. It is most appropriate for final orders where the window for urgent reconsideration under Rule 6(12)(c) has passed.

### Tier 4: Common Law Rescission for Fraud

If an *ex parte* interdict was obtained through deliberate perjury or fraud, the respondent may seek rescission under the common law. The foundational authority for this mechanism is *Childerley Estate Stores v Standard Bank* [11].

To succeed under the common law, the respondent must prove that the successful litigant was party to the fraud or perjury [12]. In *ex parte* applications, this requirement is easily met if the applicant themselves perjured the founding affidavit. 

The significant advantage of common law rescission is that it is not subject to the strict time limits associated with appeals or Rule 42 applications. An application can be brought at any time, provided the delay in bringing it is reasonably explained. Furthermore, proven fraud vitiates the entire proceeding, rendering the order a nullity from its inception [2].

### Tier 5: Section 173 Inherent Jurisdiction (The Residual Power)

Section 173 of the Constitution provides that the Constitutional Court, the Supreme Court of Appeal, and the High Courts have the inherent power to protect and regulate their own process, and to develop the common law, taking into account the interests of justice [13].

This constitutional power codifies the common law doctrine of inherent jurisdiction. However, as the Constitutional Court cautioned in *Phillips v National Director of Public Prosecutions* [14], this power must be exercised sparingly and only in clear cases. It cannot be invoked if doing so would impair the existing substantive rights of a litigant [15].

Section 173 serves as a residual mechanism. A court will follow a two-stage approach: first, identifying whether there is a lacuna in the existing rules or law that requires the court to step in; and second, assessing whether invoking the inherent power serves the interests of justice [15]. It is generally only used when the remedies in Tiers 1 through 4 are procedurally unavailable or inadequate to prevent a grave injustice.

## 3. Comparative Summary of Remedies

The following table summarizes the key distinctions between the primary procedural mechanisms for setting aside an *ex parte* interdict:

| Mechanism | Primary Application | Scope of Review | Requirement to Show "Good Cause" | Time Limitations |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|----------------------------------|------------------|
| **Rule 6(12)(c)** | Urgent *ex parte* orders (interim & final) | *De novo* hearing; court may make any order it deems fit | No | Must be brought urgently |
| **Rule 6(8)** | Interim orders with a return day (Rule *nisi*) | Consideration of whether to confirm, vary, or discharge | No | Prior to return day (24 hours' notice) |
| **Rule 42(1)(a)** | Orders erroneously sought/granted | Limited to establishing the error or non-disclosure | No | Within a reasonable time |
| **Common Law** | Orders obtained via fraud/perjury | Focuses strictly on proving the fraud | Yes (must prove fraud) | No strict limit; delay must be explained |
| **Section 173** | Residual power where lacuna exists | Broad, but must be exercised sparingly | N/A (Interests of justice test) | N/A |

## 4. Conclusion

The procedural hierarchy for setting aside *ex parte* interdicts is designed to balance the need for urgent relief with the fundamental right to be heard. Rule 6(12)(c) stands as the primary and most versatile weapon in the respondent's arsenal, offering a *de novo* reconsideration of the matter. When dealing with interim orders, Rule 6(8) anticipation provides a specific mechanism to expedite the hearing. Where material non-disclosure or procedural errors occur, Rule 42(1)(a) offers a remedy without the need to show good cause. In cases of deliberate perjury, the common law provides a powerful tool to vitiate the fraudulent order, while Section 173 of the Constitution remains the ultimate safeguard against procedural injustice.
