# Research: Shareholder Resolutions and Breaking the Catch-22

## Key Finding 1: Board vs Shareholder Powers (s66(1))
Per ENS Africa analysis of the Companies Act:
- Section 66(1): "The business and affairs of a company must be managed by or under the direction of its board"
- Shareholders CANNOT usurp management functions of the board
- BUT shareholders CAN vote on matters expressly reserved to them in the Act

## Key Finding 2: What Shareholders CAN Do
Section 65(3): "Any two shareholders of a company may propose a resolution concerning any matter in respect of which they are each entitled to exercise voting rights"

Matters shareholders CAN vote on:
- Appoint and remove directors (s68, s71)
- Amend the MOI (s16, s17)
- Approve fundamental transactions
- Approve financial assistance (s44, s45)
- Approve director remuneration (s66(8), (9))

## Key Finding 3: The Critical Distinction
Management decisions (who has access to accounts, day-to-day operations) = BOARD function
Strategic/governance decisions (appoint/remove directors, amend MOI) = SHAREHOLDER function

## Key Finding 4: BUT - Majority Directors Can Act as the Board
If the respondents are majority DIRECTORS (not just shareholders), they can:
- Call a board meeting
- Pass board resolutions by majority
- Direct management decisions including restricting access

## Key Finding 5: The Interdict Complication
The interdict may restrict the respondent directors from exercising their board powers.
BUT: the interdict gives Peter TRANSACTIONAL control, not necessarily GOVERNANCE control.
There is a difference between:
- Transactional authority (signing cheques, making payments) = what the interdict grants
- Governance authority (board resolutions, company policy) = what the Companies Act grants to the board

## Key Finding 6: Section 71 - Removal of Directors
If the respondents are majority shareholders, they can:
- Call a shareholders meeting (s61(3) - 10% threshold)
- Remove Peter as a director by ordinary resolution (s71(1))
- This would strip him of his directorial authority

BUT: the interdict may prevent this if it specifically preserves Peter's control.

## Key Finding 7: Section 45 - Financial Assistance
Section 45 governs financial assistance to directors.
Peter using company funds for his own legal fees = financial assistance to a director.
This requires:
- Board resolution (s45(2))
- Special resolution by shareholders within the previous 2 years (s45(3)(a)(ii))
- The board must be satisfied it's fair and reasonable (s45(3)(b))

If no such resolution exists, Peter's use of company funds for his own attorneys is UNLAWFUL under s45.

## CRITICAL INSIGHT: The s45 Argument
If Peter is using company funds to pay Elliott Attorneys for HIS personal litigation:
- This is "financial assistance" to a "related or inter-related person" (s45(1))
- It requires a special resolution of shareholders
- If no such resolution was passed, EVERY PAYMENT to Elliott Attorneys is unlawful
- The respondents (as majority shareholders) can REFUSE to pass such a resolution
- AND they can demand repayment of the unlawful financial assistance


## CRITICAL FINDING: Section 45 Financial Assistance

### What Section 45 Says:
Financial assistance to a director requires:
1. A special resolution of shareholders adopted within the previous 2 years
2. Board satisfaction that solvency/liquidity test is met
3. Terms must be fair and reasonable to the company

### The Exception (s45(1)(a)(ii)):
Financial assistance EXCLUDES "an accountable advance to meet legal expenses IN RELATION TO A MATTER CONCERNING THE COMPANY"

This is critical: Peter's legal fees are for HIS personal interdict against his co-directors.
The question is: is this "a matter concerning the company" or Peter's personal dispute?

If the interdict is Peter's personal action to seize control → it's NOT a company matter → s45 applies → special resolution required
If it's framed as protecting the company → it MIGHT qualify for the exception → but the company is a RESPONDENT, not the applicant

### The Devastating Argument:
The companies are RESPONDENTS in the interdict. Peter is the APPLICANT.
Peter is using company funds to sue the companies' own co-directors.
This is NOT "legal expenses in relation to a matter concerning the company."
This is financial assistance to a director for his PERSONAL litigation.

### Consequence of Non-Compliance:
Per Cliffe Dekker: "Financial assistance improperly granted in terms of section 45 can be regarded as a misappropriation of the company's money and may constitute as a ground for delinquency for directors."

The financial assistance is VOID and cannot be ratified.
The director (Peter) is personally liable for any loss under s77(3)(e)(iv).

### The Majority Shareholder Play:
If the respondents are majority shareholders:
- They have NOT passed a special resolution authorizing financial assistance to Peter
- Therefore EVERY payment from company funds to Elliott Attorneys is VOID under s45
- Peter is personally liable for repayment
- This is also a ground for declaring Peter a delinquent director under s162


## KEY FINDING: The Self-Representation Path

### Natural Persons CAN Self-Represent
The Rule in Battle only applies to COMPANIES (juristic persons).
Natural persons (individuals) CAN represent themselves in the High Court.

This means:
- Daniel and Jacqueline CAN file applications IN THEIR PERSONAL CAPACITIES
- They cannot represent the companies, but they CAN represent themselves
- Section 163 allows a DIRECTOR to apply to court for relief — as a natural person, not as the company
- Section 163 is filed by the director IN THEIR OWN NAME, not in the company's name

### The Practical Path:
1. Daniel/Jacqueline file a s163 application IN THEIR PERSONAL CAPACITIES as directors
2. They are natural persons → no Rule in Battle problem
3. The s163 application seeks relief for oppressive conduct
4. The relief sought includes:
   a. Declaration that Rynette has no authority to access company accounts
   b. Declaration that payments to Elliott Attorneys without s45 special resolution are void
   c. Order that the company fund independent legal representation for the entities
   d. Order restricting Peter's transactional control pending the rescission application

### The Board Resolution Path (for entities where respondents are majority directors):
If the respondents are majority directors of 2 of the 4 entities:
1. Call a board meeting (proper notice required)
2. Pass board resolutions by majority:
   a. Restrict Rynette's access to all company systems and bank accounts
   b. Appoint independent attorneys for the company
   c. Demand repayment of unlawful s45 financial assistance (Elliott Attorneys fees)
3. Present the resolutions to the bank
4. The bank must comply with the board resolution (it overrides individual authority)

### BUT: The Interdict May Block This
If the interdict specifically gives Peter sole transactional control:
- Board resolutions about transactions may conflict with the court order
- The respondents would need to have the interdict varied first
- BUT: governance decisions (restricting Rynette, demanding repayment) are NOT transactional
- There is a strong argument that the interdict controls BANKING transactions, not CORPORATE GOVERNANCE

### The Nuclear Option: Shareholder Resolution to Remove Peter as Director
Section 71(1): "a director may be removed by an ordinary resolution adopted at a shareholders meeting"
- Ordinary resolution = 50%+1 of votes cast
- If respondents are majority shareholders → they can remove Peter
- Once removed, Peter has no directorial authority
- The interdict gives powers to Peter AS A DIRECTOR — if he's no longer a director, the interdict may be moot
