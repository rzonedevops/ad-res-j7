# Research Notes: Procedure for Challenging Fraudulent Interdicts in SA Law

## Key Cases Found

### 1. Van Dyk v Rhodes [2024] ZAGPJHC 1323 (24 Dec 2024)
- CRITICAL: "The common law doctrine of nullity is not applicable to court orders, which derive their validity from section 165(5) of the Constitution, 1996."
- "A court order can no longer be ignored or rescinded merely upon proof that it would have been regarded as a common law nullity."
- "The ordinary principles of rescission or appeal will always apply to court orders wrongly granted, no matter what error led to their issuance."
- This means: Even if an order is fraudulently obtained, you MUST still follow proper procedure to set it aside. You cannot simply ignore it.

### 2. Mohali v Mohali [2023] ZAGPJHC 44 (24 Jan 2023)
- "No judgment of a court, no order of a Minister, can be allowed to stand if it has been obtained by fraud. Fraud unravels everything."
- The court SET ASIDE a fraudulent property transfer.

### 3. Nieman v Nieman [2026] ZANWHC 31 (16 Feb 2026)
- Ex parte order rescinded under Rule 42(1)(a) for material non-disclosure
- Respondents filed urgent interlocutory application to intervene and rescind
- Court suspended the operation of the ex parte order pending final determination

### 4. Key Principle from NDPP v Mlamuleli [2024] ZAFSHC 46
- "A final judgment, being res judicata, is not easily set aside, but the court will do so on various grounds, such as fraud, discovery of new evidence..."

## Key Legal Principles

### The Procedural Framework (Multiple Avenues):

1. **Rule 42(1)(a)** - Order "erroneously sought or erroneously granted in the absence of any party affected thereby"
   - This is the STANDARD route for ex parte orders
   - Covers orders obtained through fraud, non-disclosure, perjury
   - "Erroneously sought" includes where the applicant misled the court

2. **Rule 6(12)(c)** - Reconsideration of urgent ex parte orders
   - "A person against whom an order was granted in such person's absence in an urgent application may by notice, set down the matter for reconsideration of the order."
   - This is a SPECIFIC right for urgent ex parte orders

3. **Common Law Rescission for Fraud**
   - The court's inherent jurisdiction to set aside orders obtained through fraud
   - Based on fraus omnia corrumpit
   - NOT limited by Rule 42 time constraints
   - But per Van Dyk v Rhodes, you still need to APPLY to court

4. **Rule 30** - Irregular Proceedings
   - Where the ex parte application itself was procedurally irregular

### The Critical Distinction: VOID vs VOIDABLE

Per Van Dyk v Rhodes (2024):
- Even a fraudulently obtained order is NOT automatically void
- It must be SET ASIDE by a court through proper procedure
- Until set aside, it remains binding and enforceable
- BUT: The fraud provides the GROUNDS for setting it aside

### The Uberrima Fides Duty
- In ex parte applications, applicant owes duty of utmost good faith
- Must disclose ALL material facts, even unfavorable ones
- Non-disclosure (even if not wilful) entitles court to set aside the order
- This is a LOWER threshold than proving fraud

## The Opposition's "Uniform Rules" Argument - REBUTTAL

When the opposition argues that your submission "violates the Uniform Rules":
- They are likely arguing procedural non-compliance (wrong form, wrong rule, time limits)
- BUT: The Uniform Rules THEMSELVES provide multiple avenues for challenging fraud
- Rule 42(1)(a) is specifically designed for orders erroneously sought/granted
- Rule 6(12)(c) specifically allows reconsideration of urgent ex parte orders
- Common law fraud rescission exists ALONGSIDE the Uniform Rules
- The court's inherent jurisdiction to prevent abuse of process is constitutional (s165)

## The Correct Procedure

### Primary Route: Application under Rule 42(1)(a) read with Common Law
1. Bring a formal application on notice of motion (Rule 6(5))
2. Supported by founding affidavit setting out:
   - The fraud/perjury/non-disclosure
   - The evidence proving foreknowledge
   - Why the order should be set aside
3. Serve on all parties
4. Allow standard time for opposition
5. If urgent: invoke Rule 6(12) to dispense with forms/service

### Alternative Route: Rule 6(12)(c) Reconsideration
1. Deliver notice setting down the matter for reconsideration
2. This is a RIGHT, not a discretionary remedy
3. The court reconsiders the order de novo

### Combined Approach (Recommended):
1. Application under Rule 42(1)(a) for rescission
2. Read with common law fraud rescission (fraus omnia corrumpit)
3. Alternatively, Rule 6(12)(c) reconsideration
4. If the application itself was irregular: Rule 30

### KEY POINT: The opposition CANNOT use the Uniform Rules as a shield against fraud
- If they argue "your application doesn't comply with Rule X" - the response is:
  - The court has inherent jurisdiction to prevent fraud on the court
  - Rule 42(1)(a) specifically covers this scenario
  - Rule 6(12)(c) provides a specific right of reconsideration
  - No procedural technicality can protect a fraudulently obtained order
  - The Constitution (s165) vests judicial authority and inherent jurisdiction in courts

## Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd [2006] ZASCA 52; 2006 (4) SA 326 (SCA)

Key principles on contempt:
- Contempt requires breach that is "deliberately and mala fide"
- A deliberate disregard is not enough - must also be mala fide
- Honest belief that non-compliance is justified is incompatible with contempt intent
- Even unreasonable non-compliance, if bona fide, does not constitute contempt
- The offence is committed not by mere disregard but by "deliberate and intentional violation of the court's dignity"

CRITICAL APPLICATION: If a party genuinely believes (with evidence) that the order was obtained through fraud, their non-compliance may be bona fide and therefore NOT contempt. The fraud allegation goes to the HEART of whether contempt can be established.

## The Correct Procedural Answer

When the opposition argues "your submission violates the Uniform Rules":

THE ACTUAL CORRECT PROCEDURE IS:

1. **Application under Rule 42(1)(a)** - The primary statutory route
   - Notice of motion + founding affidavit
   - Served on all parties (Rule 42(2))
   - Sets out the fraud, perjury, non-disclosure
   - Asks court to rescind/set aside the order as "erroneously sought or erroneously granted"

2. **Alternatively/Additionally: Rule 6(12)(c)** - Reconsideration
   - Specific right for persons against whom urgent ex parte orders were granted
   - Deliver notice to set down for reconsideration
   - The court reconsiders the matter de novo

3. **Alternatively: Common law rescission for fraud**
   - Not subject to Rule 42 time limitations
   - Based on inherent jurisdiction (s165 Constitution)
   - Fraus omnia corrumpit

4. **If the application was procedurally irregular: Rule 30**

5. **Counter-application under Rule 6(7)** if responding to contempt proceedings

THE KEY INSIGHT: The Uniform Rules are NOT a barrier to challenging fraud. They PROVIDE the mechanism. The opposition's argument that "you're violating the Rules" is itself a misrepresentation if the Rules expressly allow the challenge being made.
