# Rule 7 Research Notes

## Key Distinction: TWO SEPARATE QUESTIONS

### Question 1: Who AUTHORIZES the litigation? (Board Resolution)
- Section 66(1) Companies Act 2008: "The business and affairs of a company must be managed by or under the direction of its board"
- A board resolution authorizes the DECISION to litigate
- This is an internal corporate governance matter
- The board can resolve to institute/defend proceedings

### Question 2: Who REPRESENTS the company in court? (Right of Audience)
- The "Rule in Battle" (Battle v Irish Art Promotion Centre Ltd 1968): corporate entities may only be represented in court by legal practitioners with formal right of audience
- Manong v Minister of Public Works 2010 (2) SA 167 (SCA): confirmed the rule but noted courts have "residual discretion" from inherent power to regulate proceedings
- Investec Securities v Corwil Investments (2022): non-attorney, non-director barred from representing company despite claiming board approval
- A director CANNOT represent a company in court unless they are an admitted attorney/advocate
- BUT: courts have left door open for "exceptional circumstances"

## Rule 7 Specifics

Rule 7(1): Power of attorney to act NEED NOT be filed, BUT authority can be disputed within 10 days
Rule 7(4): Every power of attorney filed by an attorney shall be signed by or on behalf of the party giving it

### The Critical Insight for the User's Question:

A board resolution can:
1. AUTHORIZE the decision to institute/defend litigation (corporate governance)
2. AUTHORIZE the appointment of attorneys/advocates to act (power of attorney)
3. AUTHORIZE a specific director to INSTRUCT attorneys on behalf of the company
4. AUTHORIZE a specific director to DEPOSE to affidavits on behalf of the company

A board resolution CANNOT:
1. Give a non-attorney the right to APPEAR in court on behalf of the company
2. Override the "Rule in Battle" requiring legal practitioners for court appearances
3. Substitute for an attorney's power of attorney under Rule 7

### BUT - The Affidavit Question (Crucial)

A director CAN:
- Depose to an affidavit on behalf of the company (they are speaking as the company's organ)
- Sign a power of attorney appointing attorneys to act
- Instruct attorneys on behalf of the company
- Authorize the litigation itself

This is different from APPEARING in court. The affidavit is the company's evidence; the court appearance is the company's representation.

## How Power of Attorney Works for Non-Attorneys

"Power of attorney" in Rule 7 context means the document authorizing an ATTORNEY to act for a party. It is NOT a general power of attorney in the commercial sense.

For a company:
1. Board resolves to litigate
2. Board resolves to appoint Attorney X
3. Director (authorized by board) signs the power of attorney appointing Attorney X
4. Attorney X files the power of attorney with the registrar (if required)
5. Attorney X represents the company in court

The board resolution is the FOUNDATION that authorizes everything else.

## CRITICAL FINDINGS FROM RESEARCH

### Ganes v Telecom Namibia Ltd 2004 (3) SA 615 (SCA) - THE KEY CASE
Justice Streicher: "The deponent to an affidavit in motion proceedings need not be authorised by the party concerned to depose to the affidavit. It is the INSTITUTION OF THE PROCEEDINGS and the PROSECUTION THEREOF which must be authorised."

### Masako v Masako [2021] ZASCA 168 (SCA)
Confirmed Ganes: A deponent to an affidavit is simply a witness stating facts within personal knowledge. No authorization needed to depose. BUT authorization IS needed to institute/prosecute proceedings.

### The Chain of Authority for a Company:

1. BOARD RESOLUTION → authorizes the DECISION to litigate (s66 Companies Act)
2. BOARD RESOLUTION → authorizes a specific director to SIGN the power of attorney appointing attorneys
3. DIRECTOR → signs the POWER OF ATTORNEY (Rule 7(4)) appointing attorneys
4. ATTORNEYS → file the power of attorney with registrar (if required under Rule 7(2)/(3))
5. ATTORNEYS → represent the company in court (right of audience)

### Rule 7(4) - The Critical Provision:
"Every power of attorney filed by an attorney shall be signed by or on behalf of the party giving it... provided that where a power of attorney is signed ON BEHALF OF the party giving it, proof of authority to sign on behalf of such party shall be produced to the registrar who shall note that fact on the said power."

THIS IS WHERE THE BOARD RESOLUTION FITS:
- The "party" is the company
- A director signs the power of attorney "on behalf of" the company
- The board resolution IS the "proof of authority to sign on behalf of such party"
- The registrar notes that proof was produced

### So the answer to the user's question:

A board resolution does NOT give a non-attorney "power of attorney" in the Rule 7 sense. Rather:
1. The board resolution authorizes the DECISION to litigate
2. The board resolution authorizes a director to ACT on behalf of the company
3. The director then SIGNS the power of attorney appointing ATTORNEYS
4. The director can also DEPOSE to affidavits (no authorization needed per Ganes)
5. The ATTORNEYS represent the company in court

A "power of attorney" under Rule 7 is specifically the document appointing an ATTORNEY to act. It is NOT a general power of attorney in the commercial sense. The board resolution is the corporate governance instrument that authorizes the director to sign that power of attorney on behalf of the company.

### Can a Director "Speak For" the Entity?

YES - in affidavits (as a deponent/witness)
NO - in court appearances (unless admitted attorney/advocate, or exceptional leave granted per Manong)

The director "speaks for" the entity by:
- Deposing to affidavits on the company's behalf
- Instructing attorneys
- Signing the power of attorney appointing attorneys
- Making decisions about the litigation strategy

The director does NOT "speak for" the entity by:
- Appearing in court
- Signing pleadings
- Filing documents with the court
- Arguing the case
