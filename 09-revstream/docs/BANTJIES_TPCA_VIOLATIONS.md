# Legal Analysis: Bantjies' Violations of the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988

## Executive Summary
Danie Bantjies' simultaneous roles as Chief Financial Officer (CFO) of The George Group and Trustee of the Faucitt Family Trust (FFT) constitute a severe, irreconcilable conflict of interest. This dual capacity directly violates the core fiduciary duties codified in the **Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988 (TPCA)** and established South African common law principles.

This document maps the specific provisions of the TPCA and relevant case law to Bantjies' documented conduct.

---

## 1. Violation of Section 9(1): The Duty of Care, Diligence, and Skill

### The Law
**Section 9(1) of the TPCA** states:
> *"A trustee shall in the performance of his duties and the exercise of his powers act with the care, diligence and skill which can reasonably be expected of a person who manages the affairs of another."*

As established in South African trust law, this imposes an **objective standard** of fiduciary duty. A trustee must act exclusively in the best interests of the beneficiaries and exercise independent judgment.

### The Violation
Bantjies cannot exercise independent judgment regarding the trust's primary asset (its R9.8 million investment in Ketoni, which flows directly into The George Group). 
- As **CFO of George Group**, his professional duty is to his employer (CEO Kevin Derrick) and the company's financial retention and growth.
- As **Trustee of FFT**, his fiduciary duty is to maximize distributions (the 90% "Dividend Sweep") for the beneficiaries (Jacqueline and Daniel Faucitt).

By accepting the trusteeship while employed as the CFO of the entity generating the trust's returns, Bantjies has placed himself in a position where he cannot exercise the required "care, diligence and skill" without breaching his duties to one party or the other.

## 2. Violation of the Common Law "No-Conflict" Rule

### The Law
South African courts have consistently upheld the strict "no-conflict" rule for fiduciaries, rooted in cases like *Robinson v Randfontein Estates Gold Mining Co Ltd 1921 AD 168*. A trustee has an absolute obligation not to permit conflicts of interest between:
1. Two competing fiduciary/professional duties.
2. The trustee's personal/professional interests and the interests of the beneficiaries.

### The Violation
Bantjies' position is the textbook definition of competing duties. The Ketoni Shareholder Agreement (Clause 1.6, Annexure B) forecasts an IRR of not less than 24% for FFT, generated entirely by George Group. If George Group faces a cash flow decision—whether to retain earnings for operations or declare a dividend that sweeps 90% to FFT—Bantjies the CFO must argue for retention, while Bantjies the Trustee must demand distribution. This is an impermissible conflict.

---

## 3. Defeating the "Independent Trustee" Requirement (Parker Case)

### The Law
In the landmark Supreme Court of Appeal case ***Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa v Parker 2005 (2) SA 77 (SCA)***, Cameron JA established the requirement for an "independent outsider" trustee in family trusts to ensure:
> *"independence of judgment on the part of the trustee – an indispensable requisite of office – as well as careful scrutiny of transactions designed to bind the trust... since an independent trustee can have no interest in concluding transactions that may prove invalid."*

### The Violation
Rynette Farrar appointed Bantjies as a Trustee of FFT in July 2024, ostensibly to fulfill the role of an independent professional trustee. However, Bantjies is the exact opposite of independent. He is financially dependent on and professionally subordinate to Kevin Derrick (CEO of George Group and sole director of Ketoni). His appointment subverts the *Parker* rule, creating a "puppet" trustee scenario designed to maintain control over the trust's assets rather than protect the beneficiaries.

---

## 4. Grounds for Removal under Section 20(1) and 20(2)(e)

### The Law
**Section 20(1)** allows the court to remove a trustee if it is "in the interests of the trust and its beneficiaries."
**Section 20(2)(e)** allows the Master to remove a trustee who "fails to perform satisfactorily any duty imposed upon the trustee by or under this Act."

In *Tijmstra NO v Blunt-MacKenzie NO 2002 (1) SA 459 (T)*, the court held: *"Whenever trust assets are endangered a trustee should be removed."* Grounds include acting as a "puppet" trustee who approves of a dominant party's wrongful conduct.

### The Violation
Bantjies' structural conflict of interest inherently endangers the trust assets. He is structurally incapable of holding The George Group (and Kevin Derrick) accountable for the R9.8 million investment and the promised 24% IRR. His removal is legally mandated under Section 20, as his continued tenure prejudices the beneficiaries.

---

## Conclusion
Danie Bantjies' acceptance and continuation of the role of FFT Trustee while serving as CFO of The George Group is a severe breach of fiduciary duty. It violates Section 9(1) of the Trust Property Control Act, the common law no-conflict rule, and the *Parker* independent trustee doctrine, rendering him liable for removal under Section 20 and potentially exposing him to personal liability for any financial prejudice suffered by the beneficiaries.
