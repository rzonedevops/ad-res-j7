# Uniform Rules of Court: Analysis of Procedural Violations

**Case:** 2025-137857 (Peter Andrew Faucitt v Jacqueline Faucitt & Others)
**Date:** 2026-03-08

## 1. Introduction

This document provides a detailed analysis of the procedural conduct of Elliott Attorneys and associated legal counsel in light of the Uniform Rules of Court. The evidence, extracted from the `revstream1` repository, reveals a consistent pattern of non-compliance and abuse of process. This analysis will proceed rule by rule, cross-referencing specific evidence to demonstrate each violation.

---

## 2. Rule 6: Applications

Rule 6 governs all applications, including the *ex parte* application that initiated this case.

### 2.1. Rule 6(12)(b): Urgency

> "...the applicant shall set forth explicitly the circumstances which it is averred render the matter urgent and the reasons why the applicant claims that applicant could not be afforded substantial redress at a hearing in due course."

The original *ex parte* application relied on a claim of urgency. However, the evidence suggests this was a fabrication. The financial matters at the heart of the dispute had been ongoing for months, as evidenced by the June 2025 correspondence between Daniel Faucitt and Danie Bantjies regarding financial irregularities. There was no sudden crisis that warranted bypassing the normal application procedures. The applicant, Peter Faucitt, could have been afforded substantial redress in due course. The failure to disclose the long-standing nature of these issues constitutes a material non-disclosure and a violation of this rule.

### 2.2. Rule 6(4)(a) & Material Non-Disclosure

The *ex parte* order was obtained without notice to the respondents. This places a duty of utmost good faith on the applicant to disclose all material facts to the court. The evidence demonstrates a catastrophic failure in this regard.

| Evidence of Non-Disclosure | Source Document |
|---|---|
| **R12,330,131.18 transferred** from supposedly protected accounts 7 days before mediation | Dan Faucitt email of 22 Sep 2025 (CCE Scans) |
| **June 2025 correspondence** regarding financial irregularities | Dan Faucitt email of 22 Sep 2025 (CCE Scans) |
| **Peter Faucitt already had full control** of the companies via the Faucitt Family Trust | Trust Deed & various company documents |

These facts, had they been disclosed, would have fundamentally altered the court's perception of the matter. The order was, therefore, "erroneously sought" and is subject to rescission under Rule 42(1)(a).

## 3. Rule 45A: Contempt of Court

> "A court may, on application, declare a person to be in contempt of court and make an appropriate order."

For a contempt finding, the applicant must prove the existence of the order, service, and non-compliance. The burden then shifts to the respondent to show their non-compliance was not **wilful and *mala fide***.

On 30 September 2025, Keegan Elliott threatened contempt proceedings against Jacqui Faucitt for attending her own company's training event. This is a clear abuse of process.

- **Not Wilful:** Jacqui, as CEO and lead trainer, was contractually and ethically obligated to conduct the training. Her attendance was not a wilful violation but a necessary business function.
- **Not *Mala Fide*:** Her intention was to ensure product safety and therapist competence, not to defy the court. As argued by Ian Levitt in his letter of 1 October 2025, her attendance caused no prejudice and was essential for the business.

Elliott's threat was a baseless act of intimidation designed to coerce and control, not a legitimate enforcement of a court order.

## 4. Rule 7: Power of Attorney

> "No person other than a party...shall be entitled to act on behalf of such party unless he is an attorney or unless he has been authorized thereto by such party by a power of attorney."

This rule was repeatedly violated, particularly concerning the authority of ENS Africa.

- **ENS acting for Peter Faucitt:** The letter from ENS to Dan Faucitt on 25 September 2025 makes it clear they were acting on Peter's instructions, not Jacqui's or Dan's. They state, "Any enforcement of the settlement agreements will not be undertaken by us but by Peter Faucitt and/or his attorneys."
- **No Power of Attorney Filed:** There is no evidence that ENS ever filed a power of attorney authorizing them to act for Jacqui or Dan. Their actions, including their withdrawal, were therefore irregular.

---

## 5. Rule 30: Irregular Proceedings

> "A party to a cause in which an irregular step has been taken by any other party may apply to court to set it aside."

The entire proceeding is littered with irregular steps:

1.  **The *ex parte* application itself:** Brought under false pretenses of urgency and with material non-disclosures.
2.  **The contempt threat:** An abuse of process and an irregular step intended to intimidate.
3.  **The conduct of ENS Africa:** Acting without a filed power of attorney and for an opposing party.
4.  **The Settlement Agreement:** Procured under duress and based on the fraudulent *ex parte* order, making it an irregular and voidable agreement.

---

## 6. Rule 42: Rescission of Orders

> "The court may...rescind or vary— (a) an order or judgment erroneously sought or erroneously granted in the absence of any party affected thereby..."

The original *ex parte* order is a textbook case for rescission under this rule.

- **Erroneously Sought:** The application was based on false claims of urgency and concealed material facts.
- **Erroneously Granted:** Had the court been aware of the full facts, it would not have granted the order.
- **In the Absence of any Party Affected:** The order was granted without the respondents being present or notified.

Furthermore, the common law ground for rescission due to fraud is clearly met. The order was obtained through a deliberate campaign of misrepresentation and is therefore **void *ab initio***.

## 7. Conclusion

The evidence demonstrates a systematic abuse of the Uniform Rules of Court by Elliott Attorneys and their client. The initial order was obtained fraudulently, and subsequent actions constituted a pattern of intimidation and irregular procedure. These violations provide extensive grounds for setting aside the original order, defending against any contempt applications, and pursuing costs on a punitive scale against the responsible parties.
