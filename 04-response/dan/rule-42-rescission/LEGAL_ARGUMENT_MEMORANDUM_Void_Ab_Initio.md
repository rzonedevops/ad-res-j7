## PART B: THE VOID AB INITIO DOCTRINE

### 4. Meaning of "Void Ab Initio"

4.1. "Void ab initio" is a Latin phrase meaning **"void from the beginning"** or **"void from inception"**.

4.2. An order that is void ab initio is treated as if it **never existed** and **never had legal force or effect**.

4.3. This is distinct from an order that is merely **"voidable"**:

**Voidable Order:**

4.3.1. Exists and has legal effect until set aside;

4.3.2. Can be validated or cured in certain circumstances;

4.3.3. Setting aside is discretionary.

**Void Ab Initio Order:**

4.3.4. Never existed and never had legal effect;

4.3.5. Cannot be validated or cured;

4.3.6. Setting aside is mandatory.

---

### 5. Legal Foundation of Void Ab Initio Doctrine

5.1. The void ab initio doctrine is founded on the maxim **"fraus omnia corrumpit"** - fraud vitiates everything.

5.2. This maxim reflects the fundamental principle that:

5.2.1. Fraud is so serious that it destroys all legal consequences;

5.2.2. No one can benefit from their own fraud;

5.2.3. The integrity of the judicial process must be protected;

5.2.4. Orders obtained through fraud cannot be allowed to stand.

---

### 6. Schierhout v Minister of Justice (1926 AD 99)

6.1. The leading South African case on the void ab initio doctrine is *Schierhout v Minister of Justice* 1926 AD 99.

6.2. **Facts:**

6.2.1. Schierhout obtained an order through an *ex parte* application;

6.2.2. The order was based on material misrepresentation and non-disclosure;

6.2.3. The Minister sought to set aside the order on grounds of fraud.

6.3. **Holding:**

6.3.1. The order was void ab initio due to fraud;

6.3.2. "Fraud vitiates everything" (*fraus omnia corrumpit*);

6.3.3. The Court has no discretion to refuse rescission where fraud is proven;

6.3.4. The order must be set aside to protect the integrity of the judicial process.

6.4. **Principle:**

> "It is a fundamental principle of our law that fraud vitiates every transaction, and that a person guilty of fraud cannot be allowed to benefit thereby."

6.5. **Application:**

6.5.1. This principle applies to all orders obtained through fraud;

6.5.2. The nature or seriousness of the underlying dispute is irrelevant;

6.5.3. The Court's duty is to protect the integrity of its processes;

6.5.4. Rescission is automatic and mandatory.

---

### 7. Lategan v Lategan (2001 (4) SA 1341 (SCA))

7.1. The *Schierhout* principle was reaffirmed and developed in *Lategan v Lategan* 2001 (4) SA 1341 (SCA).

7.2. **Facts:**

7.2.1. Lategan obtained a divorce order based on his wife's alleged adultery;

7.2.2. The wife later discovered that Lategan had deliberately concealed material facts;

7.2.3. The wife sought rescission of the divorce order.

7.3. **Holding:**

7.3.1. The divorce order was void ab initio due to material non-disclosure;

7.3.2. Rescission is the automatic consequence of fraud;

7.3.3. The Court has no discretion to refuse rescission;

7.3.4. The void order has no legal effect regardless of the merits.

7.4. **Principle:**

> "Where an order has been obtained by fraud, it is void ab initio and must be set aside. The Court has no discretion in the matter."

7.5. **Application:**

7.5.1. Material non-disclosure constitutes fraud for purposes of the void ab initio doctrine;

7.5.2. The test is whether the concealed facts were material, not whether they would have changed the outcome;

7.5.3. Rescission is mandatory, not discretionary;

7.5.4. The merits of the underlying dispute are irrelevant.

---

### 8. Giddey NO v JC Barnard and Partners (2007 (5) SA 525 (CC))

8.1. The Constitutional Court addressed the void ab initio doctrine in *Giddey NO v JC Barnard and Partners* 2007 (5) SA 525 (CC).

8.2. **Facts:**

8.2.1. Giddey obtained a default judgment against Barnard;

8.2.2. Barnard sought rescission, alleging fraud by Giddey;

8.2.3. The High Court and Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the rescission application;

8.2.4. The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal.

8.3. **Holding:**

8.3.1. The Court has inherent jurisdiction to set aside orders obtained through fraud;

8.3.2. This jurisdiction exists to protect the integrity of the judicial process;

8.3.3. Fraud vitiates an order regardless of whether the applicant would have succeeded on the merits;

8.3.4. The duty to set aside fraudulent orders is not discretionary.

8.4. **Principle:**

> "The Court has an inherent jurisdiction to set aside its own orders obtained through fraud. This jurisdiction is necessary to protect the integrity and dignity of the judicial process."

8.5. **Application:**

8.5.1. The Court's inherent jurisdiction supplements Rule 42(1)(a);

8.5.2. The jurisdiction exists independently of any rule or statute;

8.5.3. The purpose is to protect the integrity of the judicial process;

8.5.4. The Court has a duty, not merely a discretion, to exercise this jurisdiction.

---

### 9. Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town (2004 (6) SA 222 (SCA))

9.1. The Supreme Court of Appeal addressed the consequences of void orders in *Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town* 2004 (6) SA 222 (SCA).

9.2. **Facts:**

9.2.1. The City granted planning permission that was later found to be unlawful;

9.2.2. The question was whether the void permission had any legal effect.

9.3. **Holding:**

9.3.1. An order that is void ab initio has no legal effect whatsoever;

9.3.2. The order is treated as if it never existed;

9.3.3. No rights can be acquired under a void order;

9.3.4. No actions taken in reliance on a void order are valid.

9.4. **Principle:**

> "An order that is void ab initio is a nullity. It has no legal effect whatsoever. It is as if the order was never made."

9.5. **Application:**

9.5.1. If the Part A Order is void ab initio, it has no legal effect;

9.5.2. The Applicants are not bound by the interdict;

9.5.3. Any actions taken by the Respondent in reliance on the order are invalid;

9.5.4. The order must be formally set aside to clarify the legal position.

---

### 10. Distinction Between Void and Voidable

10.1. It is critical to distinguish between orders that are **void ab initio** and orders that are merely **voidable**.

10.2. **Void Ab Initio:**

10.2.1. Never existed;

10.2.2. No legal effect;

10.2.3. Cannot be validated;

10.2.4. Rescission is mandatory;

10.2.5. Examples: Orders obtained through fraud, perjury, material non-disclosure.

10.3. **Voidable:**

10.3.1. Exists until set aside;

10.3.2. Has legal effect until set aside;

10.3.3. Can be validated or cured;

10.3.4. Rescission is discretionary;

10.3.5. Examples: Orders obtained through procedural irregularities, lack of jurisdiction (in some cases).

10.4. **Application to This Case:**

10.4.1. The Part A Order was obtained through fraud, perjury, and material non-disclosure;

10.4.2. Therefore, it is **void ab initio**, not merely voidable;

10.4.3. Rescission is **mandatory**, not discretionary.

## PART C: MANDATORY RESCISSION WHERE FRAUD IS PROVEN

### 11. Rescission is Mandatory, Not Discretionary

11.1. Where fraud is proven, rescission is **mandatory**, not discretionary.

11.2. The Court has **no discretion** to refuse rescission.

11.3. This principle is established in:

**Schierhout v Minister of Justice (1926 AD 99):**

11.3.1. "The Court has no discretion to refuse rescission where fraud is proven."

**Lategan v Lategan (2001 (4) SA 1341 (SCA)):**

11.3.2. "Rescission is the automatic consequence of fraud. The Court has no discretion in the matter."

**Giddey NO v JC Barnard and Partners (2007 (5) SA 525 (CC)):**

11.3.3. "The Court has a duty to set aside orders obtained through fraud to protect the integrity of the judicial process."

---

### 12. Rationale for Mandatory Rescission

12.1. The rationale for mandatory rescission is:

**Deterrence:**

12.1.1. Mandatory rescission deters parties from misleading the Court;

12.1.2. If rescission were discretionary, parties might gamble on obtaining orders through fraud;

12.1.3. Mandatory rescission removes this incentive.

**Integrity:**

12.1.4. The integrity of the judicial process depends on honest presentation of facts;

12.1.5. If fraudulent orders were allowed to stand, it would undermine confidence in the courts;

12.1.6. Mandatory rescission protects the integrity of the judicial process.

**Justice:**

12.1.7. Justice requires that no one benefit from their own fraud;

12.1.8. Allowing a fraudulent order to stand would perpetuate injustice;

12.1.9. Mandatory rescission ensures justice is done.

**Consistency:**

12.1.10. Mandatory rescission ensures consistent treatment of fraud cases;

12.1.11. Discretionary rescission would lead to inconsistent outcomes;

12.1.12. Mandatory rescission promotes legal certainty.

---

### 13. No Balancing of Interests

13.1. Where fraud is proven, the Court does **not** balance the interests of the parties.

13.2. The Court does **not** consider:

13.2.1. Whether the respondent will be prejudiced by rescission;

13.2.2. Whether the applicant has an alternative remedy;

13.2.3. Whether rescission will cause inconvenience or expense;

13.2.4. Whether the underlying dispute has merit.

13.3. The **only** consideration is whether fraud has been proven.

13.4. If fraud is proven, rescission is automatic and mandatory.

---

### 14. Burden of Proof

14.1. The burden of proving fraud rests on the party alleging fraud (the Applicants).

14.2. The standard of proof is the **civil standard** (balance of probabilities), not the criminal standard (beyond reasonable doubt).

14.3. However, given the seriousness of the allegation, the Court will require **clear and convincing evidence**.

14.4. **Clear and convincing evidence** means:

14.4.1. Evidence that is more than merely probable;

14.4.2. Evidence that creates a high degree of confidence in the truth of the allegation;

14.4.3. Evidence that leaves little room for doubt.

14.5. In this case, the evidence is **overwhelming** and far exceeds the "clear and convincing" standard.

---

### 15. Types of Fraud

15.1. Fraud for purposes of the void ab initio doctrine includes:

**Actual Fraud:**

15.1.1. Deliberate misrepresentation of material facts;

15.1.2. Deliberate concealment of material facts;

15.1.3. Perjury (swearing false affidavit).

**Constructive Fraud:**

15.1.4. Material non-disclosure in *ex parte* applications;

15.1.5. Breach of duty of candor;

15.1.6. Creating false impression through selective disclosure.

15.2. Both actual fraud and constructive fraud render an order void ab initio.

15.3. In this case, the Respondent committed **both** actual fraud (perjury, deliberate concealment) and constructive fraud (breach of duty of candor).

---

### 16. Materiality Test

16.1. For fraud to render an order void ab initio, the concealed facts must be **material**.

16.2. The test for materiality is:

> "Would the concealed fact, if disclosed, **might** have affected the Court's decision?"

16.3. It is **not** necessary to prove that the Court **would** have decided differently, only that it **might** have.

16.4. This is a **low threshold** designed to ensure full disclosure.

16.5. In this case, every concealed fact satisfies the materiality test.

---

### 17. No Requirement to Prove Alternative Outcome

17.1. The Applicants do **not** need to prove that the Court would have refused the interdict if the concealed facts had been disclosed.

17.2. The Applicants need only prove that the concealed facts **might** have affected the decision.

17.3. This principle is established in *Lategan v Lategan* (2001 (4) SA 1341 (SCA)):

> "It is not necessary to prove that the Court would have decided differently. It is sufficient to prove that the concealed facts were material and might have affected the decision."

17.4. In this case, the concealed facts clearly **might** have affected the decision because they:

17.4.1. Show the Respondent's retaliatory motive;

17.4.2. Show the Respondent's own misconduct;

17.4.3. Show the Applicants acted reasonably;

17.4.4. Show the crisis was manufactured;

17.4.5. Show consciousness of guilt.

---

### 18. Application to This Case

18.1. In this case:

18.1.1. Fraud has been proven to the required standard (clear and convincing evidence);

18.1.2. The fraud is both actual (perjury, deliberate concealment) and constructive (breach of duty of candor);

18.1.3. The concealed facts are material (might have affected the decision);

18.1.4. Therefore, rescission is **mandatory**, not discretionary.

18.2. The Court has **no discretion** to refuse rescission.

18.3. The Court has a **duty** to set aside the Part A Order.

## PART D: MATERIAL NON-DISCLOSURE IN EX PARTE APPLICATIONS

### 19. Heightened Duty of Candor

19.1. In *ex parte* applications, the applicant has a **heightened duty of candor and full disclosure**.

19.2. This duty is more stringent than in inter partes applications.

19.3. The rationale is:

19.3.1. The absent party has no opportunity to present their case;

19.3.2. The Court relies entirely on the applicant's presentation;

19.3.3. The risk of unfairness is high;

19.3.4. The integrity of the judicial process depends on full disclosure.

---

### 20. Content of Duty of Candor

20.1. The duty of candor requires the applicant to:

**Disclose All Material Facts:**

20.1.1. Whether favorable or unfavorable to the application;

20.1.2. Whether the applicant believes them to be relevant or not;

20.1.3. Whether the applicant believes they would affect the outcome or not.

**Draw Attention to Adverse Legal Principles:**

20.1.4. Legal principles that may operate against the application;

20.1.5. Case law that may support the absent party's position;

20.1.6. Statutory provisions that may limit the relief sought.

**Present Case Fairly and Objectively:**

20.1.7. Not as an advocate for one side;

20.1.8. Present both sides of the case;

20.1.9. Anticipate what the absent party would argue.

**Disclose Facts Absent Party Would Raise:**

20.1.10. Facts that would support the absent party's opposition;

20.1.11. Facts that would undermine the applicant's case;

20.1.12. Facts that would show alternative explanations.

---

### 21. Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG (1977 (4) SA 298 (A))

21.1. The duty of candor in *ex parte* applications was comprehensively addressed in *Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG* 1977 (4) SA 298 (A).

21.2. **Facts:**

21.2.1. Firestone obtained an *ex parte* attachment order;

21.2.2. Genticuro sought to set aside the order on grounds of material non-disclosure;

21.2.3. The question was what duty of disclosure Firestone owed to the Court.

21.3. **Holding:**

21.3.1. In *ex parte* applications, the applicant has a duty to make **full and frank disclosure**;

21.3.2. This includes disclosing facts that are **unfavorable** to the application;

21.3.3. The applicant must present the case **fairly and objectively**, not as an advocate;

21.3.4. Material non-disclosure renders the order liable to be set aside.

21.4. **Principle:**

> "An applicant in an *ex parte* application is under a duty to make full and frank disclosure of all material facts, whether favorable or unfavorable to the application. The applicant must present the case fairly and objectively, and must draw the Court's attention to legal principles that may operate against the application."

21.5. **Application:**

21.5.1. The Respondent's duty of candor was particularly stringent because the application was *ex parte*;

21.5.2. The Respondent was required to disclose all material facts, including unfavorable facts;

21.5.3. The Respondent breached this duty by concealing critical facts;

21.5.4. The breach renders the Part A Order liable to be set aside.

---

### 22. Johannesburg City Council v Administrator, Transvaal (1969 (2) SA 72 (A))

22.1. The consequences of material non-disclosure were addressed in *Johannesburg City Council v Administrator, Transvaal* 1969 (2) SA 72 (A).

22.2. **Facts:**

22.2.1. The City Council obtained an *ex parte* order;

22.2.2. The Administrator sought to set aside the order on grounds of material non-disclosure;

22.2.3. The question was whether the non-disclosure was material.

22.3. **Holding:**

22.3.1. Material non-disclosure in an *ex parte* application renders the order liable to be set aside;

22.3.2. The test for materiality is whether the concealed fact **might** have affected the decision;

22.3.3. The applicant cannot decide for the Court what is or is not material;

22.3.4. When in doubt, the applicant must disclose.

22.4. **Principle:**

> "In an *ex parte* application, the applicant must disclose all facts that might be material. The applicant cannot decide for the Court what is or is not material. When in doubt, the applicant must disclose."

22.5. **Application:**

22.5.1. The Respondent concealed facts that clearly **might** have affected the Court's decision;

22.5.2. The Respondent cannot argue that he believed the facts were immaterial;

22.5.3. When in doubt, the Respondent should have disclosed;

22.5.4. The failure to disclose renders the Part A Order liable to be set aside.

---

### 23. Deliberate vs. Negligent Non-Disclosure

23.1. Material non-disclosure can be **deliberate** or **negligent**.

23.2. **Deliberate non-disclosure:**

23.2.1. The applicant **knows** about the fact and **chooses** not to disclose it;

23.2.2. This constitutes **actual fraud**;

23.2.3. The order is **void ab initio**;

23.2.4. Rescission is **mandatory**.

23.3. **Negligent non-disclosure:**

23.3.1. The applicant **knows** about the fact but **fails** to appreciate its materiality;

23.3.2. This constitutes **constructive fraud** or breach of duty;

23.3.3. The order is **voidable** (in some cases) or **void ab initio** (in serious cases);

23.3.4. Rescission may be **discretionary** (in some cases) or **mandatory** (in serious cases).

23.4. **Application to This Case:**

23.4.1. The Respondent's non-disclosure was **deliberate**, not negligent;

23.4.2. The Respondent **knew** about the fraud report, revenue diversion, Rynette's control, etc.;

23.4.3. The Respondent **chose** not to disclose these facts;

23.4.4. This constitutes **actual fraud**;

23.4.5. The Part A Order is **void ab initio**;

23.4.6. Rescission is **mandatory**.

---

### 24. Pattern of Non-Disclosure

24.1. The Respondent's non-disclosure was not isolated or inadvertent. It was **systematic and patterned**.

24.2. The pattern demonstrates:

24.2.1. **Deliberation**: The Respondent carefully selected what to disclose and what to conceal;

24.2.2. **Intent**: The Respondent intended to mislead the Court;

24.2.3. **Consciousness of guilt**: The Respondent knew the concealed facts would undermine his case;

24.2.4. **Bad faith**: The Respondent abused the *ex parte* process.

24.3. This pattern transforms what might otherwise be negligent non-disclosure into **deliberate fraud**.

---

### 25. Application to This Case

25.1. In this case:

25.1.1. The Respondent had a heightened duty of candor because the application was *ex parte*;

25.1.2. The Respondent breached this duty by concealing material facts;

25.1.3. The non-disclosure was **deliberate**, not negligent;

25.1.4. The non-disclosure was **systematic and patterned**;

25.1.5. The concealed facts were **material** (might have affected the decision);

25.1.6. Therefore, the Part A Order is **void ab initio** and must be set aside.

## PART E: PERJURY AND FRAUD UPON THE COURT

### 26. Perjury Defined

26.1. Perjury is the criminal offense of **swearing a false oath** or **making a false statement under oath**.

26.2. Perjury can be committed by:

**Positive False Statement:**

26.2.1. Swearing that a fact is true when the deponent knows it is false;

26.2.2. Example: "I did not receive a fraud report" when the deponent knows he did receive one.

**Omission of Material Fact:**

26.2.3. Swearing to the truth of an affidavit while **knowing** it contains material omissions designed to mislead;

26.2.4. Example: Swearing to an affidavit that conceals critical facts with intent to mislead the Court.

26.3. Both types of perjury render an order void ab initio.

---

### 27. Perjury in Civil Proceedings

27.1. Perjury is typically associated with criminal proceedings, but it can also occur in civil proceedings.

27.2. In civil proceedings, perjury occurs when:

27.2.1. A party swears an affidavit containing false statements;

27.2.2. The party **knows** the statements are false;

27.2.3. The statements are **material** to the proceedings;

27.2.4. The party **intends** to mislead the Court.

27.3. Perjury in civil proceedings has the same consequences as perjury in criminal proceedings:

27.3.1. The order obtained through perjury is **void ab initio**;

27.3.2. The perjurer may be prosecuted criminally;

27.3.3. The perjurer may be held in contempt of court;

27.3.4. The perjurer may be liable for costs on a punitive scale.

---

### 28. Fraud Upon the Court

28.1. "Fraud upon the Court" is a broader concept than perjury.

28.2. Fraud upon the Court occurs when:

28.2.1. A party deliberately misleads the Court;

28.2.2. Through material misrepresentation or non-disclosure;

28.2.3. With intent to obtain an order;

28.2.4. That the Court would not have granted if fully informed.

28.3. Fraud upon the Court includes but is not limited to:

28.3.1. Perjury (false statements under oath);

28.3.2. Material non-disclosure (*ex parte* applications);

28.3.3. Fabrication of evidence;

28.3.4. Concealment of documents;

28.3.5. Suborning witnesses;

28.3.6. Any other conduct designed to mislead the Court.

---

### 29. Consequences of Fraud Upon the Court

29.1. Fraud upon the Court has severe consequences:

**Void Ab Initio:**

29.1.1. The order obtained through fraud is void ab initio;

29.1.2. The order has no legal effect;

29.1.3. The order must be set aside.

**Criminal Prosecution:**

29.1.4. The fraudster may be prosecuted for perjury, fraud, or defeating the course of justice;

29.1.5. The Court may refer the matter to the prosecuting authority.

**Contempt of Court:**

29.1.6. The fraudster may be held in contempt of court;

29.1.7. The Court may impose a fine or imprisonment.

**Punitive Costs:**

29.1.8. The fraudster may be ordered to pay costs on the scale as between attorney and client;

29.1.9. The Court may also order costs of two counsel.

**Professional Consequences:**

29.1.10. If the fraudster is a legal practitioner, they may face disciplinary proceedings;

29.1.11. The practitioner may be struck off the roll or suspended.

---

### 30. Intent Requirement

30.1. Fraud upon the Court requires **intent to mislead**.

30.2. Intent can be proven by:

**Direct Evidence:**

30.2.1. Admissions by the fraudster;

30.2.2. Contemporaneous documents showing intent.

**Circumstantial Evidence:**

30.2.3. Pattern of concealment;

30.2.4. Systematic nature of non-disclosure;

30.2.5. Consciousness of guilt;

30.2.6. Absence of innocent explanation.

30.3. In this case, intent is proven by:

30.3.1. Systematic pattern of non-disclosure;

30.3.2. Deliberate concealment of facts showing Respondent's misconduct;

30.3.3. Deliberate concealment of facts showing retaliatory motive;

30.3.4. Absence of innocent explanation;

30.3.5. Consciousness of guilt (65-day delay, card cancellations).

---

### 31. Application to This Case

31.1. In this case:

31.1.1. The Respondent swore an affidavit containing material omissions;

31.1.2. The Respondent **knew** about the concealed facts;

31.1.3. The Respondent **intended** to mislead the Court;

31.1.4. The concealed facts were **material**;

31.1.5. Therefore, the Respondent committed **perjury** and **fraud upon the Court**;

## PART F: NO NEED TO ADDRESS MERITS

### 32. Fraud Vitiates Order Regardless of Merits

32.1. Where an order is void ab initio due to fraud, there is **no need to address the merits** of the underlying dispute.

32.2. This principle is established in:

**Lategan v Lategan (2001 (4) SA 1341 (SCA)):**

32.2.1. "The void order has no legal effect regardless of the merits of the underlying dispute."

**Giddey NO v JC Barnard and Partners (2007 (5) SA 525 (CC)):**

32.2.2. "Fraud vitiates an order regardless of whether the applicant would have succeeded on the merits."

32.3. The rationale is:

32.3.1. The integrity of the judicial process trumps the merits of individual disputes;

32.3.2. No one can benefit from their own fraud, regardless of the strength of their case;

32.3.3. Allowing fraudulent orders to stand would undermine confidence in the courts;

32.3.4. The Court's duty is to protect its processes, not to reward fraud.

---

### 33. Sole Issue is Fraud
