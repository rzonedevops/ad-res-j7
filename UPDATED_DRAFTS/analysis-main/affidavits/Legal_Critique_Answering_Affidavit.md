# Legal Critique: Answering Affidavit of Jacqueline Faucitt

**Prepared by:** Manus AI  
**Date:** 16 October 2025  
**Case:** Peter Andrew Faucitt v. Jacqueline Faucitt & Others (Case No: 2025-137857)

---

## Executive Summary

This critique examines the legal arguments, evidentiary support, and strategic positioning in the Answering Affidavit deposed by Jacqueline Faucitt (First Respondent) in response to an application seeking to declare her and her son, Daniel Faucitt (Second Respondent), delinquent directors. The analysis identifies significant weaknesses that could undermine the respondents' defense and provides an assessment of vulnerabilities that opposing counsel is likely to exploit.

The affidavit exhibits several critical flaws: **inadequate evidentiary support** for key assertions, a **highly problematic explanation** for a substantial financial transaction, **internal inconsistencies** regarding forensic investigations, **poorly integrated allegations** of fraud against legal professionals, and an **overly aggressive tone** that may alienate the court. These weaknesses collectively undermine the credibility of the deponents and the persuasive force of their legal arguments.

---

## 1. Structural and Procedural Weaknesses

### 1.1 Technical Error in Legal Terminology

The affidavit contains a technical error in paragraph 33, where the First Respondent correctly notes that a private company (Pty) Ltd does not have "members" as contemplated in the Close Corporations Act, but then fails to maintain consistency in terminology throughout the document. While this correction is accurate and demonstrates legal knowledge, the affidavit subsequently uses imprecise language when referring to the respondents' roles across different entity types.

**Assessment:** This is a minor weakness, but it suggests either carelessness in drafting or a lack of rigorous legal review. In a document that seeks to portray the deponents as competent and meticulous business operators, such errors are unhelpful.

### 1.2 Delay in Bringing the Application

The affidavit dedicates an entire section (paragraphs 217-221) to explaining the delay in launching the proceedings. While the explanation provided—change of legal representation, religious observances, and complexity of the matter—may be factually accurate, it highlights a procedural vulnerability.

**Weaknesses identified:**

- **Length of delay:** The delay appears substantial, though the exact timeframe is not specified. This could be characterized as acquiescence or acceptance of the interim order.
- **Blame-shifting:** The affidavit criticizes the previous attorneys (ENS) for their approach, which may appear unprofessional and could invite scrutiny of the respondents' judgment in selecting counsel.
- **Religious observances:** While legitimate, the reference to Jewish High Holy Days may be viewed as an excuse rather than a justification, particularly if the delay extended beyond the religious period.

**Strategic vulnerability:** The Applicant may argue that the delay demonstrates that the matter was not truly urgent from the respondents' perspective, undermining their claim that the ex parte order caused immediate and irreparable harm.

## 2. Evidentiary Deficiencies

### 2.1 Absence of Documentary Evidence

Throughout the affidavit, the First Respondent makes sweeping assertions that all financial transactions were legitimate, properly documented, and for bona fide business purposes. However, the affidavit fails to attach or reference specific documentary evidence to substantiate these claims.

**Examples:**

- **Paragraph 40:** Claims that payments were "supported by proper documentation and accounting records" but provides no examples or annexures.
- **Paragraph 72:** States that payments in PF9 and PF10 were "duly authorised, properly recorded" but offers no supporting invoices or authorizations.
- **Paragraph 74:** Acknowledges that supporting documents exist but claims they are not in the respondents' possession due to the interdict.

**Critical weakness:** The excuse that documents are unavailable due to the interdict is problematic. The respondents had access to the business for an extended period before the interdict and should have been able to secure copies of critical documents. Moreover, modern business practices involve digital records, cloud storage, and email trails that should be accessible regardless of physical access to premises.

**Likely attack:** Opposing counsel will argue that the failure to produce evidence suggests the evidence does not exist or would not support the respondents' version. The maxim *omnia praesumuntur contra spoliatorem* (all things are presumed against a wrongdoer who destroys evidence) may be invoked if the court infers that documents were deliberately not preserved.

### 2.2 Reliance on Promised Future Evidence

The affidavit repeatedly promises that evidence exists and can be provided, but fails to provide it in the current proceedings.

**Examples:**

- **Paragraph 70:** States that "the Second Respondent's bank statements are attached and marked as ANNEXURE" but the annexure reference is incomplete.
- **Paragraph 74:** Claims that invoices and records "can readily be substantiated" but does not substantiate them.

**Assessment:** This creates an impression of evasiveness. Courts generally expect parties to present their best evidence in their affidavits, not to promise future disclosure. The failure to do so may be interpreted as an inability to produce the evidence.

### 2.3 The "Birthday Gift" Explanation

The explanation for the R500,000 payment is perhaps the single most damaging aspect of the affidavit. The First Respondent admits that the payment was labeled "birthday gift" in the transaction description but claims this was merely a "convenient label" and that the payment was actually a director's loan.

**Paragraph 68:** "*The reference to a "birthday gift" in the transaction description was merely a convenient label for ease of reference in the company's internal payment system, and not an indication of the nature or purpose of the transaction.*"

**Critical analysis:**

This explanation is inherently implausible and raises several red flags:

1. **Inconsistent with business practice:** Legitimate director's loans are typically documented with formal loan agreements, board resolutions, or at minimum, accurate accounting entries. Labeling such a transaction as a "birthday gift" is inconsistent with professional accounting standards.

2. **Tax implications:** Describing a payment as a "gift" has significant tax implications. If it were truly a loan, it would be recorded as such for tax purposes. The mislabeling suggests either incompetence or an attempt to disguise the transaction.

3. **Credibility damage:** This explanation is likely to be viewed skeptically by the court. It invites the inference that the respondents are being dishonest about the nature of the transaction or, at best, that their record-keeping is so poor as to undermine their claims of competent management.

4. **Lack of supporting documentation:** The affidavit does not provide a loan agreement, repayment schedule, or evidence that the loan was recorded in the company's books. The promise to provide bank statements (paragraph 70) is insufficient without the underlying loan documentation.

**Strategic vulnerability:** This single issue could be used to impeach the credibility of the entire affidavit. If the court disbelieves the respondents on this point, it may be inclined to doubt their other assertions.

## 3. Internal Inconsistencies and Contradictions

### 3.1 Contradictory Position on Forensic Audit

The affidavit takes contradictory positions on the necessity and desirability of a forensic audit:

**Position 1 - Welcoming the audit:**
- **Paragraph 162:** "*We welcome and are fully supportive of an independent forensic investigation into the financial affairs of the third to sixth respondents. We are confident that such an investigation will confirm that all funds have been properly administered...*"

**Position 2 - Opposing the audit:**
- **Paragraph 114:** "*The Applicant's request to appoint necessary experts or to embark on yet another forensic investigation is unnecessary, redundant, and clearly intended to prolong litigation and inflate costs...*"

**Position 3 - Conditional acceptance:**
- **Paragraph 166:** "*The First and Second Respondents have no objection to a forensic audit being conducted, as we are confident that such an audit will vindicate our conduct... However, we do not wish for Rynette to be involved. To this end, we deny that it is either necessary or appropriate to exclude us from the financial administration of the corporations pending final adjudication.*"

**Analysis:**

These positions are fundamentally inconsistent. The respondents cannot simultaneously welcome an investigation, claim it is unnecessary and vexatious, and then seek to control its parameters. This inconsistency suggests:

- **Lack of confidence:** Despite protestations to the contrary, the respondents may not be confident that an audit will vindicate them.
- **Desire for control:** The insistence on remaining involved in financial administration during the audit suggests a desire to influence or obstruct the investigation.
- **Strategic confusion:** The contradictory positions may reflect poor legal strategy or internal disagreement about how to respond to the audit proposal.

**Likely attack:** Opposing counsel will highlight these contradictions to argue that the respondents are not genuinely transparent and have something to hide.

### 3.2 Inconsistent Characterization of the Applicant's Involvement

The affidavit characterizes the Applicant's involvement in the business in contradictory ways:

**Position 1 - Minimal involvement:**
- **Paragraph 120:** "*...his involvement in the day-to-day operations of these entities has been minimal...*"
- **Paragraph 127:** "*...he has always had access to the financial records, management accounts, and operational reports... Any alleged lack of understanding or insight is a direct result of his own lack of engagement...*"

**Position 2 - Active participant:**
- **Paragraph 69:** "*At all times, the Applicant was aware that the Second Respondent drew a director's loan, and such loans have historically been permissible and recorded in the company's books.*"
- **Paragraph 189:** "*The Applicant and I have been in business together for over thirty years...*"

**Analysis:**

The affidavit attempts to portray the Applicant as both uninvolved (and therefore lacking standing to complain) and fully informed (and therefore complicit in the alleged irregularities). This creates a logical tension. If the Applicant was minimally involved, how could he have been aware of and approved the director's loans? If he was actively involved for thirty years, why is his sudden concern about irregularities dismissed as opportunistic?

**Strategic vulnerability:** This inconsistency weakens the respondents' ability to argue either that the Applicant lacks standing or that he acquiesced to the practices he now challenges.

## 4. Substantive Legal Weaknesses

### 4.1 Failure to Address Statutory Grounds for Delinquency

The affidavit denies that the statutory grounds for a declaration of delinquency are met but does not engage substantively with the specific provisions of the Companies Act.

**Paragraph 34:** "*...the Applicant's reliance on section 162 is contrived, as the statutory grounds for delinquency or probation are not met on any factual or legal basis.*"

**Weakness:** This is a conclusory statement without detailed legal analysis. The affidavit does not:

- Identify the specific subsections of section 162 that the Applicant relies upon.
- Provide a detailed rebuttal of how the facts do not meet each element of those subsections.
- Cite relevant case law interpreting the delinquency provisions.

**Assessment:** A more robust legal argument would have engaged with the statutory criteria point-by-point, demonstrating why the respondents' conduct does not constitute gross negligence, wilful misconduct, or breach of fiduciary duty as defined in the Act and interpreted by the courts.

### 4.2 Weak Defense Against Allegations of Misappropriation

The defense against allegations of misappropriation relies heavily on denial and assertion rather than evidence and explanation.

**Paragraph 95:** "*I do not, and have never, aided or abetted my son in any alleged misconduct whatsoever. The allegations of misappropriation of funds are false, unsubstantiated, and without any factual basis.*"

**Weakness:** This is a bare denial. The affidavit does not:

- Provide a detailed account of the respondents' roles in financial decision-making.
- Explain the governance structures and approval processes for significant expenditures.
- Demonstrate through documentary evidence that the challenged transactions followed proper procedures.

**Assessment:** In the face of specific allegations supported by bank statements and transaction records, bare denials are insufficient. The respondents needed to provide a detailed, evidence-based explanation for each challenged transaction.

### 4.3 Inadequate Response to Urgency and Ex Parte Relief

The affidavit challenges the Applicant's entitlement to ex parte relief on the basis that the matter was not urgent. However, the analysis is superficial and does not engage with the legal test for urgency in South African law.

**Paragraphs 173-177:** The affidavit argues that the Applicant's delay in bringing the application (from mid-June to August) demonstrates a lack of urgency.

**Weakness:** While the delay argument has merit, the affidavit does not:

- Cite the relevant legal test for urgency (e.g., *Luna Meubel Vervaardigers (Edms) Bpk v Makin and Another (t/a Makin's Furniture Manufacturers)* 1977 (4) SA 135 (W)).
- Analyze whether the Applicant's explanation for the delay (need to gather evidence, obtain legal advice) is legally sufficient.
- Address the substantive question of whether ongoing alleged misappropriation constitutes a continuing urgency.

**Assessment:** A stronger legal argument would have engaged with the case law on urgency and demonstrated why, even accepting the Applicant's version of events, the matter did not meet the threshold for ex parte relief.

### 4.4 Insufficient Analysis of Requirements for Interdictory Relief

The affidavit denies that the requirements for interdictory relief are met but provides only cursory analysis.

**Paragraph 154:** Lists the requirements as:
- A clear right
- Irreparable harm
- The absence of an alternative remedy

**Weakness:** The affidavit does not engage substantively with each element:

- **Clear right:** Does not analyze what "right" the Applicant claims and why it is not clear.
- **Irreparable harm:** Does not explain why the alleged financial irregularities would not constitute irreparable harm to the companies.
- **Alternative remedy:** Does not identify what alternative remedies were available to the Applicant.

**Assessment:** This cursory treatment of the legal requirements for interdictory relief is a missed opportunity. A detailed analysis demonstrating why each element is not satisfied would have strengthened the respondents' case significantly.

## 5. Problematic New Allegations

### 5.1 Late Allegation of Fraud in Settlement Agreement

Paragraph 194 introduces a serious allegation that the settlement agreement (JF5) was procured by fraud, specifically that the attorneys made material changes to the agreement after the respondents' final review and misrepresented that nothing had changed.

**Critical weaknesses:**

1. **Timing:** This allegation appears very late in the affidavit and seems disconnected from the main narrative. It raises the question: why was this not discovered and raised earlier?

2. **Lack of supporting evidence:** The allegation is based solely on the deponent's recollection and assertion. There is no:
   - Copy of the draft agreement reviewed by the respondents
   - Copy of the final signed agreement showing the changes
   - Contemporaneous correspondence questioning the changes
   - Expert evidence on whether the changes were material

3. **Implausibility:** The allegation that attorneys would commit fraud by altering an agreement after review is extremely serious and inherently implausible without strong evidence. Attorneys are officers of the court and such conduct would be professional misconduct.

4. **Strategic risk:** Making such a serious allegation without solid evidence is high-risk. If the allegation is not substantiated, it could:
   - Damage the respondents' credibility
   - Expose them to adverse costs orders
   - Result in a referral to the Legal Practice Council for making unfounded allegations against legal practitioners

5. **Procedural impropriety:** If the respondents genuinely believe the settlement agreement was procured by fraud, the proper course would be to bring a separate application to set it aside, not to raise it as an ancillary issue in the answering affidavit.

**Assessment:** This allegation appears desperate and poorly conceived. It is likely to be viewed skeptically by the court and may backfire by undermining the respondents' credibility.

### 5.2 Unsubstantiated Allegations Against Rynette Farrar

The affidavit alleges that the real "rot" in the business is the unlawful conduct of the bookkeeper, Rynette Farrar, who has allegedly misappropriated funds and taken control of the entities.

**Paragraph 20:** "*...the "rot" in the business that needs to be stopped is the unlawful conduct by the businesses bookkeeper, Rynette Farrar ("Rynette"), who has misappropriated company funds and taken full control over the various business entities.*"

**Weaknesses:**

1. **No supporting evidence:** The affidavit provides no evidence of Rynette's alleged misconduct. There are no:
   - Specific transactions identified as misappropriations
   - Forensic reports or audit findings
   - Witness statements
   - Documentary evidence

2. **Inconsistency with other positions:** If Rynette has "taken full control" of the entities, how have the respondents been managing them effectively as claimed? This allegation is inconsistent with the portrayal of the respondents as competent managers.

3. **Deflection tactic:** The allegation appears to be a deflection—an attempt to shift blame to a third party without substantiation. Courts are generally skeptical of such tactics.

4. **Legal risk:** Making serious allegations of criminal conduct (misappropriation) against a third party without evidence could expose the respondents to defamation liability.

**Assessment:** This allegation is poorly developed and unsupported. It is unlikely to be given weight by the court and may be viewed as an attempt to muddy the waters.

## 6. Tone and Rhetorical Weaknesses

### 6.1 Overly Aggressive and Defensive Tone

The affidavit adopts an aggressive and defensive tone throughout, with frequent use of inflammatory language and personal attacks on the Applicant.

**Examples:**

- **Paragraph 19:** "*...the Applicant has abused the process of this Honourable Court by surreptitiously obtaining an ex parte order...*"
- **Paragraph 64:** "*...demonstrates that the Applicant's intention was not to address a legitimate concern but to execute a hostile takeover of the companies...*"
- **Paragraph 170:** "*The application is not motivated by any genuine concern for corporate governance or the survival of the companies, but rather by the Applicant's ongoing attempt to maliciously exclude myself and the Second Respondent...*"
- **Paragraph 171:** "*...vexatious, calculated solely to damage our reputations and to facilitate his hostile takeover...*"

**Weakness:** While advocacy requires forceful argument, the tone here may be counterproductive. The repeated characterization of the Applicant's motives as malicious, vexatious, and abusive may:

- Appear defensive and lacking in objectivity
- Alienate the court, which expects measured and professional submissions
- Distract from the substantive legal and factual arguments
- Invite the inference that the respondents are more focused on attacking the Applicant than defending their own conduct

**Assessment:** A more measured tone, focusing on factual rebuttal and legal argument rather than impugning motives, would likely be more persuasive.

### 6.2 Ad Hominem Attacks

The affidavit contains several ad hominem attacks that do not advance the legal arguments.

**Examples:**

- **Paragraph 43:** "*The Applicant has no technical or mathematical expertise and therefore lacks the understanding necessary to appreciate Daniel's specialised role...*"
- **Paragraph 127:** "*...he has always had access to the financial records... Any alleged lack of understanding or insight is a direct result of his own lack of engagement...*"

**Weakness:** These attacks on the Applicant's competence and engagement are not relevant to the legal issues. They may:

- Appear petty and unprofessional
- Undermine the respondents' credibility by suggesting they are more interested in scoring points than addressing the substance
- Backfire if the Applicant can demonstrate that he was actively involved or that his lack of technical expertise is irrelevant to the issues

**Assessment:** Ad hominem attacks should be avoided in legal submissions. The focus should be on the facts and the law, not on personal characteristics of the opposing party.

### 6.3 Excessive Length and Repetition

The affidavit is lengthy and repetitive, with the same points made multiple times in different sections.

**Examples:**

- The legitimacy of business expenses is asserted in paragraphs 40, 41, 72, 73, 74, 75, 77, 79, 83, and others.
- The claim that the Applicant failed to communicate his concerns is made in paragraphs 44, 45, 62, 63, and others.
- The characterization of the application as a "hostile takeover" appears in paragraphs 19, 64, 170, 177, 198, and others.

**Weakness:** Excessive length and repetition can:

- Dilute the impact of key arguments
- Frustrate the court, which must read and digest the entire document
- Suggest a lack of focus and clear legal strategy

**Assessment:** A more concise and tightly argued affidavit would be more effective. Each point should be made once, clearly and with supporting evidence, rather than repeated throughout the document.

## 7. Strategic and Tactical Weaknesses

### 7.1 Failure to Provide Alternative Explanation

Throughout the affidavit, the respondents deny the Applicant's allegations but often fail to provide a coherent alternative explanation for the facts.

**Example:** The affidavit denies that the Second Respondent acted erratically or failed to account for transactions, but does not provide a clear explanation for:

- Why subscriptions were allowed to lapse
- Why there was confusion about access to email accounts and documentation
- Why the communications in PF7 (the Applicant's annexure) appear to show difficulty in obtaining information

**Weakness:** Simply denying the Applicant's characterization without providing a plausible alternative explanation leaves a vacuum that the court may fill with inferences favorable to the Applicant.

**Assessment:** A stronger approach would have been to provide a detailed, chronological account of what actually happened, supported by documentary evidence, to demonstrate that the Applicant's interpretation is incorrect.

### 7.2 Reliance on the Applicant's Alleged Bad Faith

A significant portion of the affidavit is devoted to arguing that the Applicant acted in bad faith, with ulterior motives, and abused the court process. While these arguments may have some merit, they are not a substitute for addressing the substantive allegations.

**Weakness:** Even if the Applicant's motives were improper, the court must still determine whether the factual allegations are true and whether the legal requirements for the relief sought are met. Focusing on the Applicant's motives rather than the substance of the allegations is a weak defensive strategy.

**Assessment:** The respondents should have focused primarily on refuting the factual allegations with evidence and demonstrating that the legal requirements for delinquency are not met, rather than on attacking the Applicant's motives.

### 7.3 Inadequate Use of Confirmatory Affidavits

The affidavit mentions that confirmatory affidavits from the Second Respondent, Ian Levitt, and Natan Back are attached (paragraph 216), but does not explain what these affidavits will confirm or how they support the First Respondent's case.

**Weakness:** The failure to integrate the confirmatory affidavits into the narrative of the main affidavit suggests:

- Poor coordination between the deponents
- Lack of a coherent legal strategy
- Possible weakness in the confirmatory evidence (if it were strong, it would be highlighted)

**Assessment:** Confirmatory affidavits should be used strategically to corroborate key factual assertions and provide expert or independent evidence. The cursory reference to them in this affidavit suggests they may not add significant value.

## 9. Recommendations for Opposing Counsel

If representing the Applicant, the following lines of attack would be most effective:

1. **Focus relentlessly on the "birthday gift" transaction:** This is the Achilles' heel of the respondents' case. Emphasize the implausibility of the explanation and the lack of supporting documentation.

2. **Highlight the evidentiary vacuum:** Repeatedly note that the respondents make sweeping assertions but provide no documentary evidence. Invoke the principle that he who asserts must prove.

3. **Exploit the contradictions:** Prepare a detailed analysis of the contradictory positions on the forensic audit and the Applicant's involvement. Use these to argue that the respondents lack credibility.

4. **Emphasize the lack of legal analysis:** Point out that the respondents have not engaged substantively with the statutory criteria for delinquency or the legal requirements for interdictory relief.

5. **Characterize the fraud allegation as desperate:** Argue that the late, unsubstantiated allegation of fraud against the attorneys is a sign of desperation and should be given no weight.

6. **Use the tone against them:** Argue that the aggressive, defensive tone and personal attacks demonstrate that the respondents are more interested in attacking the Applicant than in addressing the substance of the allegations.

7. **Request adverse inferences:** Ask the court to draw adverse inferences from the failure to produce documentary evidence, particularly given the respondents' claims that such evidence exists.

## 10. Conclusion

The Answering Affidavit, while comprehensive in scope, suffers from significant legal and evidentiary weaknesses that undermine its effectiveness. The most critical flaw is the lack of documentary evidence to support the respondents' assertions, particularly the highly problematic explanation for the R500,000 "birthday gift" transaction. The internal inconsistencies regarding the forensic audit and the Applicant's involvement further damage the respondents' credibility.

The affidavit's aggressive tone and reliance on attacks on the Applicant's motives, rather than substantive engagement with the legal and factual issues, suggest a defensive posture that is unlikely to be persuasive to the court. The late introduction of a serious but unsubstantiated allegation of fraud compounds these problems and may be viewed as a sign of desperation.

Overall, while the respondents have mounted a vigorous defense, the affidavit's weaknesses provide opposing counsel with ample ammunition to attack the credibility of the deponents and the strength of their legal arguments. Without substantial documentary evidence to support their claims, the respondents face an uphill battle in defeating the application.
