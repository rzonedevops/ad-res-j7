## Part 1: Counter-Arguments on the "Birthday Gift" Transaction

### Counter-Argument 1.1: The Explanation is Convenient and After-the-Fact

**Applicant's Likely Position:**

The Respondents only provided the "director's loan" explanation after being caught. The payment narrative clearly states "birthday gift," which is what it actually was. The subsequent attempt to characterize it as a loan is a convenient fabrication designed to avoid the consequences of misappropriation. If it were truly a loan, it would have been labeled as such from the outset.

**Strength of Counter-Argument:** Medium to High

This is a strong rhetorical argument because it appeals to common sense. The court may be skeptical of an explanation that appears to contradict the contemporaneous documentary evidence (the payment narrative).

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

Address this head-on in the supplementary affidavit by acknowledging the labeling error and providing independent corroboration:

> "I acknowledge that the use of the term 'birthday gift' in the payment narrative was inappropriate and creates an unfavorable impression. However, the substance of a transaction is determined by its legal and accounting treatment, not by informal descriptions used in payment narratives. The following independent evidence demonstrates that this was, in fact, a director's loan:
>
> 1. **Accounting System Records (Annexure JF10A):** The transaction was recorded in the company's accounting system as a debit to Daniel Faucitt's director loan account on the same day the payment was made. This is a contemporaneous record created independently of any dispute.
>
> 2. **Historical Pattern (Annexure JF10B):** The director loan account ledger shows that director loans to all directors, including the Applicant, have been a routine practice for many years. This is not a novel explanation created for litigation purposes.
>
> 3. **Accountant's Confirmation (Annexure JF10C):** [If obtainable] The company's accountant has confirmed that the transaction was processed and recorded as a director's loan in accordance with standard practice.
>
> 4. **No Personal Benefit (Annexure JF4):** Daniel's bank statements show that the funds were used exclusively for [specify business-related expenses], not for personal enrichment.
>
> The Applicant, as a director with full access to the accounting system, was aware of this practice and never raised any objection until he decided to pursue this application. His selective outrage is telling."

**Additional Defensive Strategy:**

Turn the attack around by highlighting the Applicant's failure to engage:

> "If the Applicant genuinely believed this transaction was improper, he had multiple opportunities to raise it:
> - He could have questioned it at the time (July 2025)
> - He could have raised it at a board meeting
> - He could have sent a letter requesting clarification
> - He could have requested the accounting records
>
> Instead, he waited until August, obtained ex parte relief without giving us an opportunity to explain, and presented the payment narrative to the court without the accounting context. This demonstrates bad faith, not genuine concern."

### Counter-Argument 1.2: There is No Written Loan Agreement

**Applicant's Likely Position:**

If this were truly a loan, there would be a written loan agreement specifying the terms: interest rate, repayment schedule, security, etc. The absence of any such documentation proves that this was not a bona fide loan but rather an unauthorized appropriation of company funds dressed up as a loan after the fact.

**Strength of Counter-Argument:** Medium

This argument has some force, particularly if the court expects formal documentation for large transactions. However, it can be countered by showing that informal director loans are standard practice in closely-held companies.

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

Explain the practice and provide comparative evidence:

> "The Applicant's suggestion that director loans require written loan agreements demonstrates either a fundamental misunderstanding of closely-held company practices or deliberate misrepresentation. In closely-held companies where all directors are family members, director loans are routinely advanced and repaid through the director loan account system without formal written agreements. The following evidence supports this:
>
> 1. **Historical Practice (Annexure JF10B):** The director loan account ledgers show that all three directors—including the Applicant himself—have drawn director loans over the years without written agreements. The Applicant has drawn loans totaling R[X] over the past five years, none of which were documented by written agreements.
>
> 2. **Accounting Standards:** Director loan accounts are a recognized and accepted mechanism for managing director-company financial relationships. The accounting treatment—recording the loan in the director loan account—is the formal documentation of the transaction.
>
> 3. **Companies Act Compliance:** Section 45 of the Companies Act regulates financial assistance to directors but does not require written loan agreements for all director loans. The transaction complies with all statutory requirements.
>
> 4. **Accepted by Applicant for Years:** The Applicant has participated in this system for decades without objection. His sudden insistence on formalities that have never been required is inconsistent with his historical conduct and demonstrates that this application is not about proper corporate governance but about seizing control."

**Additional Defensive Strategy:**

Offer to formalize the loan retroactively if the court requires it:

> "If the court deems it appropriate, the Second Respondent is willing to execute a formal loan agreement retroactively, specifying repayment terms and interest. However, the absence of such an agreement does not transform a loan into misappropriation, particularly when the practice has been accepted by all parties for years."

**Strength of Counter-Argument:** Medium to High

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

**Strength of Counter-Argument:** Medium to High

This is a strong argument because ongoing misconduct can justify urgency even if there was some delay in bringing the application. The July payment provides a concrete example of post-discovery conduct.

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

Challenge the characterization of the July payment and the timeline:

>
> 3. **Timeline Inconsistencies:** The Applicant's timeline does not support urgency:
>    - Mid-June: Alleged discovery of irregularities
>    - 16 July: Director's loan payment (allegedly 'ongoing misconduct')
>    - 5 August: First consultation with attorneys (20 days after 'ongoing misconduct')
>    - 19 August: Ex parte order obtained (34 days after 'ongoing misconduct')
>
>    If the July payment truly demonstrated urgent, ongoing misconduct, the Applicant would have acted within days, not weeks.
>
> 4. **Self-Created Urgency:** The Applicant's delay in investigating and acting created whatever urgency existed. He cannot benefit from his own delay by characterizing it as 'investigation time.'

> 5. **No Evidence of Escalation:** The Applicant has not presented any evidence that misconduct was 'escalating.' The expenses he challenges are consistent with historical patterns, not increasing.

**Additional Defensive Strategy:**

Cite case law on self-created urgency:

> "The principle is well-established that an applicant cannot create urgency through his own delay and then rely on that urgency to justify ex parte relief. [Cite relevant case law.] The Applicant's conduct falls squarely within this principle."

## Part 5: Counter-Arguments on Duty of Candor

### Counter-Argument 5.1: The Respondents' Version was Not Material

**Applicant's Likely Position:**

The Respondents argue that the Applicant failed to disclose material facts in the ex parte application. However, the Respondents' version of events—even if true—would not have changed the outcome. The core facts (large unexplained payments, lack of documentation, refusal to provide information) justified the ex parte relief regardless of the Respondents' explanations. Therefore, there was no material non-disclosure.

**Strength of Counter-Argument:** Medium

This argument has some force if the court believes that the Applicant's evidence was sufficient to justify relief even without hearing the Respondents' version. However, it can be countered by showing that the Respondents' version fundamentally changes the picture.

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

Demonstrate that the Respondents' version is materially different and would have changed the outcome:

> "The Applicant's suggestion that our version of events is immaterial is demonstrably false. Had the court been aware of the following facts, it would not have granted ex parte relief:
>
> 1. **Director Loan Practice:** The court was not informed that director loans are a routine practice in the companies, that all directors (including the Applicant) have drawn such loans, and that the R500,000 payment was properly recorded as a loan in the accounting system. This is highly material because it transforms the transaction from apparent misappropriation to standard practice.
>
> 2. **Business Justification for Expenses:** The court was not informed of the business justification for the IT and systems expenses, the industry context, or the return on investment. The Applicant presented these expenses as facially improper without providing the context that would have shown them to be necessary and appropriate.
>
> 3. **Applicant's Delay:** The court was not informed that the Applicant waited two months between alleged discovery and application, during which time he took no action to prevent alleged ongoing misconduct. This is material to the assessment of urgency.
>
> 4. **Applicant's Failure to Engage:** The court was not informed that the Applicant never once communicated his concerns to us or requested an explanation before seeking ex parte relief. This is material to the assessment of whether ex parte relief was necessary.
>
> 5. **Applicant's Historical Acceptance:** The court was not informed that the Applicant had been involved in the management of the companies for decades and had accepted the very practices he now characterizes as misconduct. This is material to the assessment of whether the conduct was truly improper.
>
> Had the court been aware of these facts, it would have required the Applicant to give notice and allow us to respond before granting relief. The Applicant's failure to disclose these material facts constitutes a breach of the duty of candor and justifies setting aside the ex parte order."

The Respondents cannot fault the Applicant for failing to disclose their version when they had refused to communicate it to him.

> "The Applicant's claim that we 'refused to provide information' is false:

> 2. **Applicant's Access:** The Applicant, as a director, had full access to all company records, including the accounting system, bank statements, and supporting documentation. He did not need to request information—he could have accessed it himself. His failure to review the accounting records before making allegations of misappropriation demonstrates recklessness, not diligence.
>
> 3. **Duty to Investigate:** The duty of candor requires the applicant to conduct reasonable investigation before making ex parte application. The Applicant could have reviewed the accounting records, consulted with the accountant, or examined the supporting documentation. His failure to do so before alleging misconduct is a breach of the duty of candor.
>
> 4. **Selective Presentation:** Even based on the information available to him, the Applicant selectively presented facts to create a misleading impression. For example, he presented the 'birthday gift' payment narrative without disclosing that he had access to the accounting system and could have verified how the transaction was actually recorded.

**Alternative Response if the Applicant did request information:**

If the Applicant can produce evidence that he requested information and the Respondents refused, the response must be different:

> "The Applicant's characterization of our response to his requests is misleading:
>
> 1. **Nature of Requests:** The Applicant's requests were [characterize them—e.g., 'vague and sweeping,' 'accusatory and hostile,' 'made without proper context']. We responded by [describe the response].
>
> 2. **Timing:** The Applicant's requests were made [specify timing]. This did not provide adequate time for us to compile comprehensive responses before he filed the ex parte application.
>
> 3. **Good Faith:** We were attempting to respond in good faith, but the Applicant did not wait for our responses before seeking ex parte relief. This demonstrates that his requests were not genuine attempts to obtain information but rather steps to create a record to justify ex parte relief.

> Even if we were slow to respond to the Applicant's requests, this does not justify ex parte relief. The appropriate response would have been to file an application on notice, compelling us to respond. The Applicant's decision to proceed ex parte was a tactical choice to prevent us from responding, not a necessity."

## Part 6: Counter-Arguments on Legal Grounds

### Counter-Argument 6.1: Delinquency Does Not Require Criminal Conduct

**Applicant's Likely Position:**

The Respondents argue that they have not committed any criminal offense and therefore cannot be declared delinquent. This is a misunderstanding of the law. Section 162 of the Companies Act allows for delinquency declarations based on gross negligence, wilful misconduct, or breach of fiduciary duty, none of which require criminal conduct. The Respondents' mismanagement and self-dealing constitute gross negligence and breach of fiduciary duty, which are sufficient grounds for a delinquency declaration.

**Strength of Counter-Argument:** High

This is a strong legal argument because the Applicant is correct that delinquency does not require criminal conduct. The respondents must not overstate the legal requirements.

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

Acknowledge the legal standard but argue that it is not met:

> "I acknowledge that a delinquency declaration under section 162 of the Companies Act does not require proof of criminal conduct. However, it does require proof of gross negligence, wilful misconduct, wilful breach of fiduciary duty, or similar serious misconduct. The Applicant has not established any of these grounds:
>
> 1. **No Gross Negligence:** Gross negligence requires a serious departure from the standard of care expected of a reasonable director. The evidence demonstrates that we have managed the companies competently and successfully for [X years], growing revenue, expanding operations, and maintaining profitability. Specific management decisions that the Applicant disagrees with do not constitute gross negligence.
>
> 2. **No Wilful Misconduct:** Wilful misconduct requires intentional wrongdoing. The Applicant has not identified any intentional wrongdoing. At most, he alleges that certain expenses were excessive or that certain transactions were improperly documented. Even if these allegations were true (which they are not), they would constitute errors of judgment, not wilful misconduct.
>
> 3. **No Breach of Fiduciary Duty:** Fiduciary duty requires directors to act in good faith in the best interests of the company. All of our actions have been taken in good faith to advance the business interests of the companies. The IT investments, systems development, and operational expenses were all directed toward growing and improving the business.
>
> 4. **High Standard:** The courts have emphasized that delinquency declarations are serious remedies that should be granted only in clear cases of serious misconduct. [Cite relevant case law.] The Applicant's vague allegations of excessive spending and poor documentation do not meet this high standard.
>
> The Applicant has not established any ground for a delinquency declaration. His application should be dismissed."

### Counter-Argument 6.2: Interdictory Relief Does Not Require Irreparable Harm

**Applicant's Likely Position:**

The Respondents argue that the Applicant has not shown irreparable harm. However, the protection of company assets from ongoing misappropriation is a sufficient basis for interdictory relief even if the harm can ultimately be quantified in monetary terms. The courts recognize that allowing directors to continue misappropriating funds while litigation proceeds is unacceptable, and interdictory relief is appropriate to prevent such ongoing harm.

**Strength of Counter-Argument:** Medium

This argument has some force because courts do grant interdictory relief to prevent ongoing financial misconduct even when the harm is ultimately compensable in damages.

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

Argue that there is no ongoing misconduct to prevent:

> "The Applicant's argument assumes that there is ongoing misconduct to prevent. There is not:
>
> 1. **No Misconduct:** As I have demonstrated, the challenged transactions were legitimate business expenses and properly documented director loans. There is no misconduct, ongoing or otherwise.
>
> 2. **No Evidence of Future Harm:** The Applicant has not presented any evidence that we intend to engage in any improper conduct in the future. His application is based entirely on retrospective allegations about past transactions.
>
> 3. **Alternative Remedies:** Even if the Applicant's allegations were true (which they are not), there are adequate alternative remedies short of excluding us from management:
>    - Requiring dual signatures for large transactions
>    - Requiring board approval for transactions above a certain threshold
>    - Appointing an independent financial monitor
>    - Requiring regular financial reporting to all directors
>
> 4. **Disproportionate Relief:** The relief granted—complete exclusion from management—is grossly disproportionate to the alleged harm. The Applicant has seized total control of the companies based on allegations of excessive spending and poor documentation. This is not a measured response to protect company assets; it is a hostile takeover.
>
> 5. **Harm to Companies:** The interdictory relief is causing far more harm to the companies than any alleged misconduct. By excluding the very persons who have successfully managed the companies for decades, the Applicant is jeopardizing ongoing operations, client relationships, and business opportunities.
>
> The requirements for interdictory relief are not met. The order should be discharged."

## Part 7: Strategic Counter-Arguments

### Counter-Argument 7.1: The Respondents are Attacking the Applicant to Deflect from Their Own Conduct

**Applicant's Likely Position:**

The Respondents' affidavit is a transparent attempt to deflect attention from their own misconduct by attacking the Applicant. Rather than addressing the substantive allegations, they focus on the Applicant's motives, his delay in bringing the application, and his alleged failure to communicate. This is a classic deflection tactic. The court should focus on the Respondents' conduct, not on the Applicant's decision-making about when and how to bring the application.

**Strength of Counter-Argument:** Medium

This argument has some force because extensive focus on the Applicant's conduct can appear to be deflection. However, it can be countered by showing that the Applicant's conduct is directly relevant to the issues.

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

Explain why the Applicant's conduct is relevant:

> "The Applicant characterizes our analysis of his conduct as 'deflection,' but his conduct is directly relevant to the key issues in this application:
>
> 1. **Urgency:** The Applicant's delay between discovery and application is directly relevant to whether the matter was truly urgent and whether ex parte relief was justified. If the Applicant did not act urgently, the matter was not urgent.
>
> 2. **Duty of Candor:** The Applicant's failure to communicate with us before seeking ex parte relief is directly relevant to whether he breached the duty of candor by failing to present our version to the court.
>
> 3. **Good Faith:** The Applicant's pattern of conduct—obtaining ex parte relief, seizing exclusive control, refusing to engage—is directly relevant to whether this application is a good faith effort to protect the companies or a bad faith attempt to seize control.
>
> 4. **Credibility:** The Applicant's historical acceptance of the very practices he now characterizes as misconduct is directly relevant to his credibility and to whether the conduct was truly improper.
>
> We have also addressed the substantive allegations in detail, providing documentary evidence to refute each allegation. The Applicant's suggestion that we have not addressed the substance is false.
>
> The Applicant's conduct is not a peripheral issue—it is central to the court's assessment of this application. His attempt to characterize our analysis as 'deflection' is itself a deflection from his own improper conduct."

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

### Counter-Argument 8.2: The Respondents Have Not Provided Key Documents

**Applicant's Likely Position:**

Despite the Respondents' extensive annexures, they have conspicuously failed to provide certain key documents that would be expected if their version were true. For example, they have not provided board resolutions approving the director loans, written loan agreements, formal expense approval documentation, or complete audit trails. **Strength of Counter-Argument:** Medium to High

This is a strong argument if there are genuinely missing documents that would be expected in a well-run company. The response depends on whether such documents exist and, if not, why not.

**Recommended Pre-emptive Response:**

Explain the absence of formal documentation:

> "The Applicant suggests that the absence of certain formal documents (board resolutions, written loan agreements, etc.) undermines our version. This suggestion reflects a misunderstanding of how closely-held family companies operate:
>
> 1. **Informal Governance:** In closely-held companies where all directors are family members and work together daily, formal board resolutions and written agreements are often not prepared for routine transactions. Decisions are made through informal discussion and consensus, and are implemented through the accounting system.
>
> 2. **Historical Practice:** This has been the practice in these companies for decades, including throughout the period when the Applicant was actively involved in management. He never insisted on formal documentation for his own director loans or for other routine transactions. His sudden insistence on formalities that have never been required is inconsistent and opportunistic.

> 3. **Accounting Records as Documentation:** The formal documentation of transactions is the accounting system. When a director loan is recorded in the accounting system, that is the formal record of the transaction. Additional written agreements are not required by law and have never been the practice in these companies.
>
> 4. **Willing to Formalize:** If the court deems it appropriate, we are willing to prepare formal documentation retroactively. However, the absence of such documentation does not mean the transactions were improper—it means they were conducted in accordance with the informal practices that have always governed these companies.
>
> The Applicant is attempting to impose standards of formal corporate governance that have never applied to these companies and that he himself never followed. This is a transparent attempt to create the appearance of impropriety where none exists."
