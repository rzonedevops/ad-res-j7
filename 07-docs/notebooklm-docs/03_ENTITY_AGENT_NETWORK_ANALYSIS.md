## 3. The 11-Dimensional Agent State Model

To ensure a deep and consistent analysis, each key agent is assessed across eleven dimensions. This model provides a structured framework for understanding agent behavior.

| Dimension | Description | Application in this Case |
|:---|:---|:---|
| 1. Knowledge State | The agent's expertise, information access, and awareness. | e.g., Daniel's expert knowledge of IT and finance; Peter's knowledge of Bantjes' debt. |
| 2. Intent State | The agent's motivations, strategic objectives, and desired outcomes. | e.g., Peter's intent to control the Trust; Daniel's intent to expose fraud. |
| 3. Capability State | The agent's resources, authority, and operational capacity to act. | e.g., Peter's absolute powers as Main Trustee; Rynette's control over bank accounts. |
| 4. Opportunity State | Situational factors and circumstances that enable or constrain the agent's actions. | e.g., The *ex parte* application created an opportunity for the curator appointment. |
| 5. Benefit State | The financial, strategic, or personal gains the agent stands to achieve. | e.g., The ZAR 18.75M Ketoni payout is a major benefit state for the controller of the Trust. |
| 6. Legal-Awareness State | The agent's understanding of the legal implications and compliance obligations of their actions. | e.g., Jacqueline's high awareness of her duties as POPIA Information Officer and EU RP. |
| 7. Strategic-Coordination State | The agent's collaboration, alliances, and coordinated actions with other agents. | e.g., The coordinated actions of Peter, Rynette, and Bantjes in the interdict and financial maneuvers. |
| 8. Regulatory-Compliance State | The agent's status and obligations concerning relevant laws and regulations. | e.g., The company's compliance state with POPIA and EU regulations is jeopardized by the interdict. |
| 9. Network-Position State | The agent's centrality, influence, and structural role within the overall network. | e.g., Peter's central position as the ultimate controller of the Trust and initiator of the conflict. |
| 10. Temporal-Causation State | The agent's role in the causal chain of events and the timing of their actions. | e.g., The timing of the interdict (Aug 13) as a direct causal result of the whistleblower reports (June 6-10). |
| 11. Evidence-Provenance State | The connection of the agent's actions and claims to the available evidence, including its verification level. | e.g., Claims about Bantjes' debt are linked to loan agreements with a high verification status. |

The Trust, in turn, owns and controls the key operating companies like **RegimA Worldwide**. Peter leverages **Mr. Bantjes** for legal control and **Rynette** for financial control.

This suggests a system designed to concentrate wealth away from the core business and its beneficiaries.

- **Conflict of Interest Network:** The most significant conflict is **Bantjes'** debt to **Peter**, which compromises his duties as Trustee and curator. A secondary conflict is **Rynette's** son's company, **Adderory**, supplying goods to **RegimA** while Rynette controlled the payments.

- **Action-Reaction Sequence (Retaliation):** The network clearly shows a causal chain:
    1.  **Daniel & Jax** uncover and confront Peter about fraud (May 15).
    2.  **Daniel** files official whistleblower reports (June 6-10).
    3.  **Peter** engages in immediate retaliation (card cancellation, June 11).
    4.  **Peter** engages in delayed, strategic retaliation via the legal system, using **Bantjes** to file the interdict (Aug 13).
